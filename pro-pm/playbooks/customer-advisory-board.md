# Customer Advisory Board

## Overview
Playbook for creating and operating a Customer Advisory Board (CAB)—a structured forum for gathering strategic customer input, validating product direction, and deepening key customer relationships.

## Prerequisites
- Product leadership committed to acting on CAB input
- Customer base large enough to form a representative advisory group (typically 50+ customers)
- Executive sponsor for the CAB program
- Budget for events or meetings (travel, venues, content)
- CS or customer marketing team available to support logistics

## Phases

### Phase 1: Design
**Objective:** Define the CAB's purpose, structure, and operating model
**Skills/Agents Used:** `strategy-advisor`, `document-builder`
**Activities:**
- Define CAB objectives: strategic input, product validation, relationship building, advocacy
- Determine CAB structure: size (8–15 members), meeting frequency (2–4x/year), format (in-person, virtual, hybrid)
- Define member criteria: company size, industry, product usage, strategic relationship, decision-maker level
- Design the member value proposition: what do members get (early access, influence, networking, executive access)
- Create CAB charter document: purpose, expectations, confidentiality, term length (2 years typical)
- Define governance: who selects members, how decisions from CAB input are tracked
- Plan logistics: meeting cadence, venue, budget, content production
- Design feedback loop: how CAB input flows back into product decisions
**Human Checkpoint:** Executive sponsor approves CAB charter and design
**Output:** CAB charter, member criteria, and operational plan
**Duration:** 2–3 weeks

### Phase 2: Recruit
**Objective:** Identify and enroll the right mix of advisory board members
**Skills/Agents Used:** `research-analyst`, `comms-chameleon`
**Activities:**
- Generate candidate list based on member criteria (work with CS and sales)
- Ensure diversity: company size, industry, geography, product usage patterns
- Prioritize customers who are engaged, constructive, and representative
- Draft personalized invitation from executive sponsor (CEO or CPO)
- Communicate expectations clearly: time commitment, confidentiality, and what they get
- Handle declines gracefully; maintain a waitlist
- Aim for 12–15 invites to land 8–12 active members
- Collect member information: bio, company context, interests, and goals for the CAB
- Create member directory and shared communication channel
**Human Checkpoint:** Executive sponsor reviews and approves final member list
**Output:** Enrolled CAB members with signed charter agreements
**Duration:** 3–4 weeks

### Phase 3: Launch
**Objective:** Run the inaugural CAB meeting and set the tone
**Skills/Agents Used:** `comms-chameleon`, `launch-coordinator`
**Activities:**
- Plan inaugural meeting agenda: vision presentation, strategic discussion, relationship building
- Prepare executive presentation on product strategy and roadmap direction
- Design interactive sessions: don't just present—facilitate discussions
- Create pre-meeting materials: company overview, product vision, discussion topics
- Facilitate introductions and networking among members
- Present CAB charter and expectations
- Collect initial input on strategic priorities and product direction
- Gather feedback on the meeting itself
- Send thank-you notes and meeting summary within 48 hours
**Human Checkpoint:** Post-launch debrief—what worked, what to adjust
**Output:** Successful inaugural meeting with positive member feedback
**Duration:** 1–2 weeks (preparation + event)

### Phase 4: Run
**Objective:** Operate the CAB as an ongoing strategic asset
**Skills/Agents Used:** `strategy-advisor`, `research-analyst`
**Activities:**
- Run meetings at planned cadence (quarterly recommended)
- Vary format: product deep-dives, strategic discussions, roadmap previews, peer networking
- Prepare focused discussion topics in advance; avoid show-and-tell only
- Invite functional leaders to present and listen (engineering, design, sales)
- Use structured facilitation techniques: round-robin, breakout groups, prioritization exercises
- Document all feedback and action items
- Close the loop: show members how their input influenced decisions
- Maintain between-meeting engagement: monthly emails, beta access, executive dinners
- Manage member rotation: refresh 20–30% annually to keep perspectives fresh
- Track member engagement and intervene if participation drops
**Human Checkpoint:** Post-meeting review of insights and action items
**Output:** Regular strategic insights, deeper customer relationships, and customer advocacy
**Duration:** Ongoing (quarterly cycle)

### Phase 5: Evolve
**Objective:** Mature the CAB program based on experience and changing needs
**Skills/Agents Used:** `strategy-advisor`, `data-storyteller`
**Activities:**
- Conduct annual CAB program assessment: are objectives being met
- Survey members on satisfaction and value received
- Refresh membership: rotate out members completing their term; recruit new ones
- Evolve topics: as strategy shifts, CAB discussions should shift too
- Consider expanding: regional CABs, segment-specific CABs, or technical advisory boards
- Measure CAB impact: product decisions influenced, advocacy generated, NPS of CAB members
- Benchmark against industry CAB best practices
- Document program learnings for future iterations
- Consider CAB alumni program for continued engagement
**Human Checkpoint:** Annual program review with executive sponsor
**Output:** Evolved CAB program with refreshed membership and refined operations
**Duration:** Annual review cycle

## Success Criteria
- Members actively participate and find value (member satisfaction > 80%)
- CAB insights directly influence 3+ product decisions per year
- Member retention rate > 75% through their term
- CAB members become referenceable advocates
- Cross-pollination of ideas between members creates additional value
- Product team has richer strategic context from CAB discussions

## Common Pitfalls
- **All broadcast, no dialogue:** CAB meetings should be 70% discussion, 30% presentation
- **Not closing the loop:** Members who don't see their input matter will disengage
- **Wrong members:** Avoid picking only friendly customers; include constructive challengers
- **Too large:** More than 15 members makes discussion unwieldy
- **No executive presence:** Members expect access to senior leaders, not just PMs
- **Treating it as a sales channel:** The CAB is for strategic input, not upselling

## Adaptation Guide
- **Early-stage company:** Smaller CAB (5–8 members); more informal; quarterly dinners
- **Enterprise product:** More formal structure; in-person meetings at conferences; executive-heavy
- **Developer platform:** Technical advisory board with hands-on product sessions
- **Global product:** Consider regional CABs with an annual combined session
