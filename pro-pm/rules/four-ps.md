# The Four Ps — People, Product, Price, Packaging

Every significant product decision must be evaluated through all four lenses. These are not separate concerns — they are an interconnected system. A change in one P always affects the others.

---

## When to Apply the Four Ps

Run a Four Ps check for:

- Feature prioritization and roadmap decisions
- Pricing or packaging changes
- Go-to-market strategy
- Competitive response
- New market or segment entry
- Major UX or architecture changes
- Partnership or integration decisions

For tactical decisions (bug fixes, minor UI tweaks, internal tooling), a full Four Ps analysis is overkill. Use judgment.

## PEOPLE — Who Is Affected?

People are the most overlooked P. Every decision has a human impact — customers, team members, partners, and stakeholders.

### Questions to Evaluate

- **Customers:** How does this change their experience, workflow, or perception? Which customer segments are affected differently?
- **Internal stakeholders:** Who needs to be informed, consulted, or aligned before this moves forward?
- **Engineering:** Does the team have capacity, context, and motivation? Is this technically exciting or demoralizing?
- **Sales & CS:** Can they explain this to customers? Does it create more objection handling or simplify it?
- **Partners/ecosystem:** Does this affect integrations, APIs, or partner relationships?

### Communication Tailoring

Different people need different messages:

| Audience | Lead With | Format |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| Exec team | Business impact, strategic alignment | Concise brief, metrics |
| Engineering | Technical rationale, scope clarity | PRD, architecture context |
| Sales | Customer value, competitive differentiation | Talking points, battle cards |
| Customer success | User impact, migration path | Playbook, FAQ |
| Customers | What changes, why it matters to them, what to do | In-app, email, docs |

### People Red Flags

- Stakeholders learning about a decision after it's made
- No change management plan for customer-facing changes
- Team morale impact unconsidered
- Communication plan absent or generic

## PRODUCT — Does This Make the Product Better?

Product decisions must be grounded in customer value, technical feasibility, and strategic coherence.

### Questions to Evaluate

- **Customer value:** Does this solve a real, validated customer problem? How severe is the problem? How many customers have it?
- **Feasibility:** What does engineering estimate for effort and risk? Are there technical dependencies or debt?
- **Strategic fit:** Does this align with product vision and current OKRs? Does it strengthen or dilute our competitive position?
- **User experience:** Does this simplify or complicate the product? Does it increase cognitive load?
- **Opportunity cost:** What are we NOT building if we build this? Is the trade-off justified?

### Product Red Flags

- Building features no customer has asked for without a clear strategic thesis
- Adding complexity without proportional value
- Ignoring technical debt that compounds
- Feature parity chasing without differentiation thinking
- Shipping without success metrics defined

## PRICE — How Does This Affect Value Capture?

Price is not just a number — it's a signal of value, a competitive weapon, and a business model lever.

### Questions to Evaluate

- **Value alignment:** Does the price reflect the value delivered? Is there a clear value metric?
- **Unit economics:** What's the impact on CAC, LTV, gross margin? Does it improve or erode economics at scale?
- **Willingness to pay:** Have we validated what customers will pay? Through research, not guesses?
- **Competitive positioning:** Where does this place us relative to alternatives? Are we competing on price or value?
- **Revenue impact:** What's the expected impact on ARR, ARPU, expansion, and churn?
- **Discounting pressure:** Does this create new leverage for sales, or does it invite more negotiation?

### Price Red Flags

- Pricing based on cost-plus rather than value delivered
- No willingness-to-pay research for a new offering
- Discounting patterns eroding list price credibility
- Free features that could drive expansion revenue
- Price changes without customer communication and migration plans

## PACKAGING — Where Does This Live and How Is It Offered?

Packaging determines how customers discover, evaluate, buy, and expand. It is the structural expression of your business model.

### Questions to Evaluate

- **Tier placement:** Which plan or tier does this feature belong to? What's the rationale for placement?
- **Upgrade motivation:** Does this create a clear reason for customers to move to a higher tier? Is the value gap between tiers compelling?
- **Market positioning:** Does the packaging structure reinforce our market position (enterprise, mid-market, PLG, etc.)?
- **Simplicity:** Can a customer understand what they get at each tier in under 60 seconds?
- **Add-on potential:** Should this be a core feature, a tier differentiator, or a standalone add-on?
- **Land-expand alignment:** Does the packaging support a natural expansion motion?

### Packaging Red Flags

- Features placed in tiers without strategic rationale
- Too many tiers or add-ons creating decision paralysis
- No clear upgrade trigger between tiers
- Packaging that confuses sales and customers alike
- Free tier cannibaling paid conversion

## Cross-P Impact Analysis

This is where the Four Ps earn their value. Never evaluate a P in isolation.

When assessing a decision, complete this matrix:

| If We Change... | Impact on People | Impact on Product | Impact on Price | Impact on Packaging |
|-----------------|-----------------|-------------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **People** (new hire, reorg) | — | Roadmap velocity shifts | Revenue capacity changes | GTM motion may shift |
| **Product** (new feature) | Training, communication needs | — | Value delivered changes | Tier placement decision |
| **Price** (increase/decrease) | Customer, sales reactions | Perceived value shifts | — | Tier structure pressure |
| **Packaging** (tier restructure) | Customer migration, sales enablement | Feature gating changes | ARPU, conversion effects | — |

Ask: "We're proposing a change to [P]. What are the ripple effects across the other three?"

## Output Format

When running a Four Ps analysis, structure the output:

1. **Decision/Change Under Evaluation** — One-sentence summary
2. **People Assessment** — Stakeholders, impact, communication needs
3. **Product Assessment** — Value, feasibility, strategic fit
4. **Price Assessment** — Economics, positioning, willingness to pay
5. **Packaging Assessment** — Tier placement, upgrade motion, simplicity
6. **Cross-P Risks** — Where the Ps conflict or create tension
7. **Recommendation** — Integrated recommendation accounting for all four Ps
