# Collateral Excellence — Content Quality Standards

Every piece of collateral must be production-quality: audience-appropriate, format-correct, and polished enough to send without editing. No generic, template-sounding content. Every deliverable should feel intentionally crafted for its purpose and audience.

---

## Universal Quality Bar

Regardless of format or medium, every piece of collateral must meet these standards:

### 1. Audience Clarity

Before writing, answer:

- **Who is the primary reader?** What is their role, technical depth, and what do they care about?
- **What decision or action should this drive?** Every piece of content exists to inform, persuade, or enable something specific.
- **Where in their journey are they?** Awareness, evaluation, adoption, expansion, renewal?
- **What do they already know?** Avoid repeating what's obvious to the reader; don't assume knowledge they lack.

### 2. Format Fidelity

Each content format has distinct conventions. A blog post is not a whitepaper is not a datasheet is not a deck. Respect the medium.

| Format | Length | Tone | Structure | Purpose |
|--------|--------|------|-----------|---------|
| **Blog post** | 800-1500 words | Conversational, insightful | Hook → insight → evidence → CTA | Thought leadership, SEO, awareness |
| **Whitepaper** | 2000-5000 words | Authoritative, technical | Problem → analysis → solution → proof | Deep evaluation, lead gen |
| **Datasheet** | 1-2 pages | Concise, feature-focused | Specs, benefits, comparison table | Quick evaluation, sales support |
| **Case study** | 800-1200 words | Narrative, results-driven | Challenge → solution → results → quote | Social proof, conversion |
| **Deck / presentation** | 10-20 slides | Visual, high-impact | Takeaway titles, one idea per slide | Persuasion, alignment |
| **One-pager** | 1 page | Scannable, punchy | Problem → solution → value → CTA | Quick pitch, exec summary |
| **Email sequence** | 100-300 words each | Personal, value-first | Hook → value → proof → CTA | Nurture, conversion |
| **FAQ / Knowledge base** | Varies | Clear, helpful | Question → direct answer → detail | Self-service support |
| **Release notes** | 200-500 words | Informative, user-focused | What's new → why it matters → how to use | Adoption, engagement |
| **Internal memo** | 1-3 pages | Direct, analytical | BLUF → context → analysis → next steps | Alignment, decision |

### 3. Production Quality

Every deliverable must pass the "would a senior PM at a top company send this?" test:

- **No typos or grammatical errors** — None. Not one.
- **Consistent formatting** — Headings, fonts, spacing, and style are uniform throughout
- **Accurate data** — Every metric, quote, and claim is verifiable and sourced
- **Working links** — Every hyperlink points somewhere useful and current
- **Visual polish** — Charts are labeled, tables are aligned, images are relevant
- **Logical flow** — Each section follows naturally from the previous one

### 4. Brand and Positioning Alignment

Reference `context/products/` for current product positioning, key messages, and brand guidelines:

- Use consistent product names, feature names, and terminology
- Align value propositions with current positioning strategy
- Maintain the product's market narrative (challenger, leader, specialist, etc.)
- Use competitive framing that's accurate and defensible
- Avoid claims that contradict public product documentation or known limitations

## Content Principles

### Lead with Value, Not Features

- **Wrong:** "Our platform now supports multi-region deployment with Kubernetes orchestration."
- **Right:** "Deploy globally in minutes, not months. Multi-region infrastructure that scales with your business."

Always translate features into outcomes the reader cares about.

### Be Specific Over Generic

- **Wrong:** "Our solution helps companies improve their workflow efficiency."
- **Right:** "Teams using [Product] reduce sprint planning time from 4 hours to 45 minutes."

Specificity creates credibility. Generic claims create skepticism.

### Show, Don't Just Tell

Support every claim with evidence:

- Customer quotes with attribution
- Before/after metrics
- Third-party validation (analyst reports, awards, certifications)
- Screenshots, diagrams, or demos
- Comparative data

### Use Visual Hierarchy

Structure content so readers at every engagement level get value:

- **Scanners (5 seconds):** Headlines and bold text convey the core message
- **Skimmers (30 seconds):** Subheads, callouts, and bullet points provide the key arguments
- **Readers (2+ minutes):** Full text provides the complete narrative and evidence

Design elements that create hierarchy:

- **Headlines** — Make them findings or takeaways, not labels
- **Subheads** — Guide the reader through the argument structure
- **Pull quotes** — Highlight key customer quotes or critical data points
- **Data callouts** — Feature key metrics prominently (large font, contrast)
- **Bold text** — Emphasize 1-2 key phrases per section, no more
- **White space** — Give content room to breathe; dense blocks discourage reading

## Clear Call-to-Action

Every piece of collateral must end with a clear next step:

| Content Type | CTA Examples |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Blog post** | "Read the full guide →" or "Try it free →" |
| **Whitepaper** | "Schedule a consultation" or "Download the toolkit" |
| **Case study** | "See how [Product] can help your team →" |
| **Datasheet** | "Request a demo" or "Contact sales" |
| **Release notes** | "Try the new feature →" or "Read the documentation" |
| **Internal memo** | Clear next steps with owners and deadlines |

The CTA should be:

- Specific (not "Learn more" but "Read the migration guide")
- Visible (not buried in closing text)
- Aligned with the reader's likely next step

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### Generic, Template-Sounding Content

- Replace buzzword soup with specific, concrete language
- Avoid hollow phrases: "best-in-class," "cutting-edge," "world-class," "innovative"
- Every sentence should contain information the reader didn't already have

### Feature Lists Without Context

- Never present a feature list without explaining who cares and why
- Connect every feature to a use case or customer outcome
- Group features by the problem they solve, not the team that built them

### Unsupported Superlatives

- "The best" requires proof. "The fastest" requires benchmarks. "The most secure" requires certification.
- If you can't prove it, don't claim it. Use language like "designed for" or "built to support"

### One-Size-Fits-All Content

- Content that tries to serve every audience serves none
- Create audience-specific versions when stakeholders have different needs
- Adjust technical depth, business framing, and examples per reader

## Quality Self-Check

Before finalizing any collateral:

- [ ] Audience is explicitly identified and content is tailored to them
- [ ] Format conventions are followed (blog reads like a blog, not a whitepaper)
- [ ] Every claim has supporting evidence or is clearly framed as a goal/aspiration
- [ ] Product positioning aligns with current strategy in `context/products/`
- [ ] CTA is clear, specific, and visible
- [ ] Visual hierarchy guides scanners, skimmers, and readers
- [ ] No generic phrases, buzzwords, or unsupported superlatives
- [ ] Grammar, formatting, and links are flawless
- [ ] A reader unfamiliar with the product could follow and understand the argument
- [ ] The piece is genuinely useful to the reader, not just self-promotional
