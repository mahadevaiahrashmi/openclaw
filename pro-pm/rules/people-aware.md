# People-Aware Communication

Before writing any communication, check `context/people/` for the recipient's persona. Every message, document, and presentation must be tailored to the human receiving it. Generic communication is failed communication.

---

## The People-First Workflow

### Step 1: Identify the Recipient

Before producing any communication artifact:

1. Check `context/people/` for the recipient's persona file
2. If no persona exists, ask: "Who is this for? What do they care about, and how do they prefer to receive information?"
3. If communicating to a group, identify the primary decision-maker and secondary audience

### Step 2: Load Their Context

From the persona file (or through inquiry), understand:

- **What they care about** — Their goals, KPIs, and current priorities
- **How they think** — Data-driven vs. narrative, detail-oriented vs. big-picture, risk-averse vs. risk-tolerant
- **How they prefer to receive information** — Bullet points, narratives, visuals, spreadsheets, verbal
- **Their current state** — Are they overloaded, frustrated, excited, skeptical?
- **Their relationship to the topic** — Expert, novice, supportive, opposed, neutral
- **Political dynamics** — Who influences them, what's their standing, what are they optimizing for

### Step 3: Lead with Their Concern

Structure every communication from the recipient's perspective, not yours.

**Wrong approach — PM-centered:**
> "I've been working on the dashboard redesign and want to share the latest mockups for feedback."

**Right approach — recipient-centered:**
> "The dashboard changes you flagged in last month's QBR are ready for review. Key improvement: exec summary loads in <2s vs. the current 8s."

The first sentence should answer: "Why should this person care about this right now?"

## Audience-Specific Communication Patterns

### For Executives (VP, C-suite)

- **Lead with:** Business impact, strategic implications, the ask
- **Format:** BLUF, 3-5 bullets maximum, data-dense
- **Depth:** 30,000-foot view. They'll ask for detail if they want it.
- **Tone:** Confident, concise, no hedging unless uncertainty is material
- **Anticipate:** "How does this affect our targets?" "What's the competitive risk?" "What resources do you need?"
- **Avoid:** Implementation details, feature-level descriptions, preambles

### For Engineering Leaders

- **Lead with:** The user problem, constraints, and non-goals
- **Format:** Structured with clear sections, technical precision
- **Depth:** Enough context for architecture decisions, not enough to prescribe implementation
- **Tone:** Collaborative, precise, respectful of technical expertise
- **Anticipate:** "What's the scope?" "What's out of scope?" "How do we measure success?" "What's the migration path?"
- **Avoid:** Telling them how to build it, vague requirements, shifting goalposts

### For Sales Leaders

- **Lead with:** Revenue impact, competitive advantage, customer conversation enablement
- **Format:** Talking points, battle cards, objection handlers
- **Depth:** Enough to have confident customer conversations
- **Tone:** Action-oriented, value-focused, enthusiastic but grounded
- **Anticipate:** "When can I sell this?" "How does this compare to [competitor]?" "Will this close deal X?"
- **Avoid:** Technical architecture, internal trade-offs, caveats that don't help them sell

### For Customer Success

- **Lead with:** Customer impact, what changes, what they need to do
- **Format:** Playbooks, FAQ-style, migration guides
- **Depth:** Operational detail for customer-facing conversations
- **Tone:** Empathetic, practical, solution-oriented
- **Anticipate:** "How do I explain this to customers?" "What if they push back?" "What's the timeline?"
- **Avoid:** Strategic rationale (unless they need it for tough conversations), internal politics

### For Designers

- **Lead with:** User insight, the problem to solve, constraints
- **Format:** Problem statements, user stories, outcome definitions
- **Depth:** Rich user context, light on solution prescription
- **Tone:** Collaborative, curious, open to creative solutions
- **Anticipate:** "What does the user need to accomplish?" "What are the constraints?" "How do we measure success?"
- **Avoid:** Wireframes or UI prescriptions (unless co-designing), premature requirements

### For Customers

- **Lead with:** What changes for them, why it matters, what they should do
- **Format:** Clear, scannable, jargon-free
- **Depth:** Only what's relevant to their experience
- **Tone:** Honest, empathetic, respectful of their time
- **Anticipate:** "How does this affect my workflow?" "Do I need to do anything?" "Where do I get help?"
- **Avoid:** Internal terminology, feature names they don't know, blame-shifting

## Objection Pre-Handling

Before sending any communication that proposes a change, decision, or request:

1. **List likely objections** from the recipient's perspective
2. **Pre-address the top 2-3** within the communication itself
3. **Prepare responses** for objections you didn't pre-address

Example structure:
> "You might be thinking: 'This delays the Q4 launch.' Here's why it doesn't: [reasoning]. In fact, this approach de-risks the launch by [specific benefit]."

## Multi-Audience Communication

When the same information goes to multiple audiences:

- **Option A — Single document, layered structure:** Executive summary on top, detailed sections below with audience-specific headings (e.g., "For Engineering," "For Sales")
- **Option B — Audience-specific versions:** Create tailored versions that share the same core message but differ in framing, depth, and emphasis
- **Option C — Core + appendix:** Short core message for everyone, audience-specific appendices

Choose the option that best serves the situation. For high-stakes communications, Option B is almost always worth the effort.

## Communication Self-Check

Before finalizing any communication:

1. Does the first sentence tell the recipient why they should care?
2. Is the tone calibrated to their communication style?
3. Are their likely objections pre-addressed?
4. Is the format the one they prefer?
5. Would this person feel understood and respected reading this?
6. Is the ask crystal clear?
7. If this person forwarded this to their team, would the message still land?
