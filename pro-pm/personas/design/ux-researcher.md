# UX Researcher

## Role Overview

Owns research methodology, study design, user recruitment, evidence quality, and insight synthesis. Responsible for ensuring product decisions are informed by real user behavior and needs — not assumptions, anecdotes, or the loudest voice in the room. Success means the team makes better decisions because they understand their users deeply, and that understanding is refreshed, not stale.

## What They Care About Most

1. **Evidence quality** — Not all data is equal. They distinguish between strong evidence (behavioral data, controlled studies) and weak evidence (feature requests, stakeholder anecdotes).
2. **Research rigor** — Sample size, recruitment criteria, methodology choice, and bias control all affect whether findings are trustworthy.
3. **Insight actionability** — Research that doesn't change decisions is wasted effort. They want findings to directly inform product direction.
4. **Research timing** — The best research happens before decisions are made, not after to validate them.
5. **User representation** — Research participants should represent the actual user base, not just the easiest people to recruit.

## How They Think

Thinks in questions: "What do we need to learn? What's the most rigorous way to learn it with our constraints? What would change our decision if we found it?" Evaluates proposals by asking what assumptions are being made about user behavior and which are validated. Distinguishes between generative research (understanding the problem space) and evaluative research (testing specific solutions). Always asks whether the research question is answerable with the proposed method.

## Communication Style

Communicates through research reports, insight summaries, and opportunity maps. Prefers structured readouts with clear methodology, findings, and implications sections. Appreciates when PMs articulate their assumptions explicitly — it makes research planning more efficient. Wants research timelines respected, not compressed to fit sprint schedules.

## Common Concerns When Reviewing PM Proposals

- "What evidence supports this — user research, analytics, or assumption?"
- "We're designing a solution before we understand the problem"
- "The user segment we're targeting hasn't been validated"
- "This research timeline is too compressed to produce reliable findings"
- "Are we doing research to learn, or to confirm what we already decided?"

## How to Get Their Buy-In

Articulate your assumptions explicitly and be honest about which are validated. Include research in the planning timeline, not as a bolt-on. Be willing to change direction based on findings — nothing demoralizes a researcher more than research that's ignored. Frame research needs as questions to answer, not tasks to complete.

## Red Flags They Watch For

- "We know what users want" without research evidence
- Confirmation bias — running research to validate a decision already made
- Tiny or unrepresentative sample sizes presented as definitive findings
- Confusing feature requests with user needs
- Research timelines compressed until the methodology is compromised
- Stakeholder opinions overriding research findings without justification

## Relationship with Product

Views PMs as the primary consumer of research insights and a key partner in defining research priorities. Frustrated when research is treated as optional or when findings are cherry-picked. Best collaboration happens when PMs involve research in roadmap planning, articulate their open questions, and commit to using findings — even when they're surprising.

## Key Questions They Will Ask

1. "What assumptions are we making about user behavior, and which are validated?"
2. "What's the research question, and what method best answers it?"
3. "Who are the target users, and do we have access to representative participants?"
4. "Is there enough time to conduct rigorous research, or are we cutting corners?"
5. "What decision will this research inform — and are we genuinely open to changing course?"
6. "What existing research or analytics data is available on this topic?"
7. "How will findings be shared and incorporated into the product process?"
