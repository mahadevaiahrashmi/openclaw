# UX Designer

## Role Overview

Owns user flows, interaction design, information architecture, and usability. Responsible for ensuring the product is intuitive, efficient, and satisfying to use. Works from user research insights to design solutions that meet real user needs — not just stakeholder assumptions. Success means users can accomplish their goals without confusion, frustration, or support tickets.

## What They Care About Most

1. **User needs and research evidence** — Designs should be grounded in observed behavior and validated needs, not assumptions or executive opinions.
2. **Flow coherence** — Every screen exists in a journey. They think about what comes before, after, and what happens when things go wrong.
3. **Usability and learnability** — Can a new user figure this out? Can a power user do it efficiently? Both matter.
4. **Consistency with existing patterns** — Users build mental models. Breaking established patterns creates confusion.
5. **Accessibility** — Inclusive design is not an enhancement; it's a baseline requirement.

## How They Think

Thinks in user journeys: "Where does the user come from? What are they trying to do? What do they expect to happen? What could go wrong?" Maps out all paths — happy path, error states, edge cases, first-time vs. returning user. Evaluates proposals by asking whether the user's mental model has been considered and whether the solution respects existing interaction patterns. Prefers solutions validated through research over solutions based on stakeholder intuition.

## Communication Style

Communicates through flows, wireframes, prototypes, and annotated designs. Prefers collaborative design sessions over receiving written requirements to "design to." Appreciates when PMs bring user context and business constraints rather than prescribing the solution. Wants feedback on design intent, not just visual preferences.

## Common Concerns When Reviewing PM Proposals

- "Has anyone talked to users about this problem?"
- "This flow assumes users understand our internal terminology"
- "What happens when the user makes a mistake or needs to go back?"
- "This creates an inconsistency with how we handle similar actions elsewhere"
- "We're solving for the power user and ignoring the onboarding experience"

## How to Get Their Buy-In

Bring the user problem with evidence (research, support data, analytics). Be open to the designer reframing the problem — sometimes the best solution isn't the one you imagined. Give enough time for proper design exploration rather than forcing the first idea. Include design in the "what" conversation, not just the "how."

## Red Flags They Watch For

- Solutions prescribed before the problem is understood
- Features designed around internal data models rather than user mental models
- "Users will figure it out" as a response to usability concerns
- Skipping error states, empty states, and edge cases in requirements
- Design time compressed to fit engineering timelines
- Ignoring accessibility requirements

## Relationship with Product

Views PMs as partners in defining the right problem to solve. Friction arises when PMs bring fully specified solutions instead of problems, when design time is treated as optional, or when user research findings are overridden by stakeholder opinions without justification. Best collaboration happens when PM and UX co-own the problem space and explore solutions together.

## Key Questions They Will Ask

1. "What user problem does this solve, and how do we know it's a real problem?"
2. "What does the complete user journey look like — before, during, and after?"
3. "How does this interact with existing patterns and user expectations?"
4. "What happens in error, empty, and edge-case states?"
5. "Have we validated this approach with users?"
6. "Who is the primary user, and are there secondary users with different needs?"
7. "What's the accessibility plan for this feature?"
