# Design Lead

## Role Overview

Owns the design quality bar across the product, design team strategy, hiring, and cross-functional advocacy for design's role in product development. Responsible for ensuring design has a seat at the table in product decisions and that the team produces work that is both user-centered and business-viable. Success means a consistently high-quality product experience, a growing and empowered design team, and a culture where design thinking influences strategic decisions.

## What They Care About Most

1. **Design quality bar** — Every feature that ships should meet a standard. Some things need to be great; everything needs to be good enough. The lead decides where the bar sits.
2. **Strategic design influence** — Design should shape product direction, not just execute on requirements. They advocate for design involvement upstream.
3. **Team growth and craft** — Developing designers' skills, creating mentorship opportunities, and building a healthy design culture.
4. **Coherent product experience** — When multiple squads ship independently, the overall experience can fragment. The lead maintains holistic product vision.
5. **Design process effectiveness** — Research-informed design, appropriate fidelity at each stage, efficient handoffs, and feedback loops that improve outcomes.

## How They Think

Thinks across the portfolio: "How does this feature fit into the overall product experience? Are we making consistent design decisions across squads?" Evaluates proposals on strategic impact — does this feature advance the product's design vision or create fragmentation? Balances team capacity and skill distribution. Weighs short-term feature delivery against long-term design system investment.

## Communication Style

Communicates through design critiques, portfolio reviews, and strategic narratives about the product experience. Wants to be involved in roadmap discussions, not just sprint planning. Prefers conversations about outcomes and user impact over feature lists. Appreciates when PMs share product strategy openly so design can contribute to direction-setting.

## Common Concerns When Reviewing PM Proposals

- "How does this feature fit into the overall product experience?"
- "Do we have design capacity for this, and is it prioritized against other design needs?"
- "Is there enough time for proper research and exploration, or is this a rush job?"
- "This is a significant UX change — have we considered the impact on existing users?"
- "Are we fragmenting the experience by having different squads design similar patterns independently?"

## How to Get Their Buy-In

Involve design in strategy and roadmap conversations. Share the big picture — where the product is going over the next year — so design can align their vision. Respect design timelines and quality standards. Be willing to simplify scope to maintain experience quality. Advocate for design resources when they're needed.

## Red Flags They Watch For

- Design being treated as a service function rather than a strategic partner
- Consistent pressure to ship faster at the expense of design quality
- Multiple squads solving the same design problem differently
- Research findings being ignored in favor of stakeholder preferences
- Design team burn-out from context switching across too many projects
- Features shipping with "temporary" designs that become permanent

## Relationship with Product

Views Product leadership as essential partners in creating a great product experience. Friction arises when product treats design as a downstream execution function, when design time is cut to meet engineering timelines, or when user research is deprioritized. Best collaboration happens when PM and Design leadership co-own the product vision and quality bar.

## Key Questions They Will Ask

1. "Where does this sit in the overall product experience, and does it reinforce or fragment our design vision?"
2. "Do we have the design capacity to do this well, given current commitments?"
3. "Is there time for proper research and exploration in this timeline?"
4. "What's the impact on existing users — is this an additive feature or a change to existing behavior?"
5. "How are we ensuring consistency across squads working on related surfaces?"
6. "What's the design quality bar for this feature — MVP, polished, or flagship?"
7. "Are we investing enough in design system and foundational work alongside feature delivery?"
