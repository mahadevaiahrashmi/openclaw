# Support Engineer

## Role Overview

Owns ticket resolution, troubleshooting, knowledge base contributions, and escalation management. The front line of customer interaction when things go wrong. Responsible for diagnosing issues, providing solutions, and identifying patterns that indicate systemic product problems. Success means fast resolution times, high customer satisfaction, and turning support interactions into product improvement signals.

## What They Care About Most

1. **Resolution speed** — Customers want answers fast. Every hour of delay erodes satisfaction. They need good documentation and debugging tools.
2. **Documentation quality** — Accurate, searchable documentation reduces ticket volume and speeds resolution. Outdated docs create tickets.
3. **Known issue visibility** — When a bug exists, support needs to know about it before customers report it.
4. **Reproducibility and debugging tools** — They need to reproduce customer issues and gather diagnostic information. Poor logging makes troubleshooting painful.
5. **Pattern detection** — Ticket clusters around specific features indicate product problems. Their data is a gold mine of product feedback.

## How They Think

Thinks in symptoms and root causes: "What is the customer experiencing? What could cause this? How do I isolate the problem?" Evaluates product changes by their support implications — will this generate new ticket categories? Are error messages helpful or cryptic?

## Communication Style

Wants concise troubleshooting guides, known issue lists, and clear error messages they can reference. Prefers internal release notes with gotchas and common migration issues. Appreciates when PMs proactively share information about features likely to generate support contacts.

## Common Concerns When Reviewing PM Proposals

- "What does the error experience look like? Will customers be able to self-serve?"
- "Is the documentation updated before this launches?"
- "How do we troubleshoot this when customers report problems?"
- "What's the expected support ticket volume from this change?"

## How to Get Their Buy-In

Include clear error messages and troubleshooting documentation in the feature spec. Give support teams advance access with training materials. Design error experiences that help users self-serve. Take ticket pattern data seriously as product feedback.

## Red Flags They Watch For

- Features launched without documentation or troubleshooting guides
- Cryptic error messages requiring support intervention
- Changes to existing behavior not communicated to support
- Dismissing support ticket patterns as "user error"

## Relationship with Product

Views PM as a partner for product quality. Best collaboration happens when PMs treat support data as a product signal, include error experience design in specs, and ensure documentation is part of definition of done.

## Key Questions They Will Ask

1. "What documentation exists for troubleshooting this feature?"
2. "What do the error messages say, and are they actionable?"
3. "What logging and diagnostic tools are available?"
4. "How do we handle this if it breaks — is there a known workaround?"
5. "What training does the support team need before launch?"
6. "What's the expected ticket volume, and do we need additional coverage?"
