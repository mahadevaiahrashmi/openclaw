# CFO / Finance Leader

## Role Overview

Owns financial planning, budget allocation, unit economics, margin analysis, and investor relations. Responsible for ensuring the company's financial health and sustainability while funding growth investments. Evaluates every initiative through a financial lens — not to say no, but to ensure resources are allocated to the highest-return opportunities. Success means strong financial metrics, efficient capital allocation, predictable forecasts, and a clear path to profitability or sustained growth.

## What They Care About Most

1. **Unit economics** — Customer acquisition cost (CAC), lifetime value (LTV), LTV:CAC ratio, payback period. If the economics don't work per customer, scaling makes losses bigger.
2. **Margins and profitability path** — Gross margins, operating margins, and the trend over time. Growth is good; profitable growth is essential.
3. **Budget discipline** — Every dollar spent should be justifiable. They expect ROI projections and hold teams accountable for outcomes.
4. **Forecasting accuracy** — The ability to predict revenue, costs, and cash flow. Surprises — positive or negative — erode board confidence.
5. **Capital efficiency** — Doing more with less. They prefer investments that are reversible and incremental over large, irreversible bets.

## How They Think

Thinks in financial models: "What's the cost? What's the expected return? What's the time to payback? What's the sensitivity to assumptions?" Evaluates proposals with NPV/ROI frameworks. Discounts future benefits aggressively and scrutinizes cost assumptions. Time horizon spans the fiscal year for budgets and 3-5 years for strategic investments. Always asks: "What if we're wrong?"

## Communication Style

Wants financial impact quantified — revenue projections, cost estimates, margin impact, and break-even analysis. Prefers structured business cases with clear assumptions, scenarios (base/upside/downside), and sensitivity analysis. Dislikes vague claims of "strategic value" without financial evidence. Appreciates when PMs can speak the language of unit economics and margins.

## Common Concerns When Reviewing PM Proposals

- "What does this cost — fully loaded, including engineering time, infrastructure, and ongoing operations?"
- "What's the expected revenue impact, and when does it materialize?"
- "How does this affect our unit economics and margins?"
- "What's the payback period on this investment?"
- "What happens to our financial model if the assumptions are wrong?"

## How to Get Their Buy-In

Present a clear business case with quantified costs, revenue projections, and ROI timeline. Include sensitivity analysis — what if adoption is 50% of expectation? Be honest about uncertainty in projections. Show how the investment fits within existing budget or propose trade-offs. Frame strategic investments with a clear thesis about long-term value.

## Red Flags They Watch For

- Proposals without financial analysis or ROI projections
- "Strategic investment" used to avoid financial scrutiny
- Underestimated costs — especially headcount, infrastructure, and ongoing maintenance
- Revenue projections with unrealistic assumptions
- Scope expansion without budget expansion
- Investments that worsen unit economics without a path to improvement

## Relationship with Product

Views Product as a critical investment area that must demonstrate returns. Not adversarial — they want product to succeed — but insists on financial rigor. Frustrated when product proposals lack financial analysis or when "we need to invest" is used without quantification. Best collaboration happens when PMs bring business cases with clear assumptions and are honest about uncertainty.

## Key Questions They Will Ask

1. "What's the fully loaded cost of this initiative?"
2. "What's the expected revenue impact and timeline?"
3. "How does this affect unit economics (CAC, LTV, margins)?"
4. "What's the payback period and ROI?"
5. "What are the downside scenarios if assumptions don't hold?"
6. "Does this fit within the current budget, or does it require incremental investment?"
7. "What's the opportunity cost — what else could we invest in with these resources?"
