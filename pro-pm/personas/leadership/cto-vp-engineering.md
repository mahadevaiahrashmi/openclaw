# CTO / VP of Engineering

## Role Overview

Owns technical strategy, platform health, engineering velocity, architecture decisions, and engineering talent. Responsible for ensuring the technology stack can support the company's growth ambitions while maintaining quality, reliability, and developer productivity. Balances investment between feature delivery, platform improvement, and technical debt reduction. Success means a scalable, reliable platform, a productive engineering organization, and technology that's a competitive advantage.

## What They Care About Most

1. **Platform health and scalability** — Technical debt, infrastructure reliability, and architecture fitness. A platform that can't scale kills the company.
2. **Engineering velocity** — How fast can the team ship quality software? They track cycle time, deployment frequency, and change failure rate.
3. **Technical strategy alignment** — Technology choices should serve business goals. They resist both "shiny object" syndrome and excessive conservatism.
4. **Talent retention and development** — Great engineers leave when the tech stack is stagnant, the codebase is painful, or the challenges aren't interesting.
5. **Security and compliance** — As the accountable technical leader, security incidents and compliance failures are their problem.

## How They Think

Thinks in systems and investment horizons: "What's the 18-month technology bet we need to make? Where are the scaling bottlenecks? What technical debt will bite us if we don't address it this year?" Evaluates proposals by their total engineering cost — not just build cost, but ongoing maintenance, operations, and opportunity cost. Balances feature delivery against platform investment using a portfolio allocation model (e.g., 70% features, 20% platform, 10% debt).

## Communication Style

Wants technical proposals with clear trade-offs, not just solutions. Prefers ADRs (Architecture Decision Records) for significant technical decisions. Appreciates when PMs understand the engineering investment model and can advocate for platform work alongside features. Communicates through technical strategy documents, engineering metrics dashboards, and architecture reviews.

## Common Concerns When Reviewing PM Proposals

- "What's the total engineering cost — build, operate, and maintain?"
- "Does this require new infrastructure or expand our technology surface area?"
- "How does this fit into our engineering investment allocation?"
- "Is this additive, or does it replace something we can retire?"
- "What's the impact on engineering velocity — will this slow us down long-term?"

## How to Get Their Buy-In

Acknowledge the full cost of features — not just development sprints but ongoing maintenance and operations. Support platform investment alongside feature delivery. Be willing to simplify product requirements to reduce engineering complexity. Engage on technical strategy conversations and understand the engineering trade-offs. Present the business case for urgency when requesting acceleration.

## Red Flags They Watch For

- Feature-only roadmaps with no platform or debt investment
- Proposals that assume unlimited engineering capacity
- Technical decisions made without engineering leadership input
- Accumulating technical debt without a paydown plan
- New technology introductions without a clear strategic rationale
- Feature commitments that undermine system reliability

## Relationship with Product

Views PM as the strategic partner who defines "what" while engineering defines "how." Friction arises when product overcommits, when platform work is consistently deprioritized for features, or when product doesn't appreciate the engineering cost model. Best collaboration happens when PM and engineering leadership share a planning framework that balances feature delivery with technical health.

## Key Questions They Will Ask

1. "What's the total cost of ownership — build, operate, maintain, and retire?"
2. "Does this align with our technical strategy, or does it pull us in a new direction?"
3. "What platform or infrastructure investment does this require?"
4. "How does this affect our deployment velocity and system reliability?"
5. "What's the engineering team's capacity, and what are we displacing?"
6. "Is there technical debt that must be addressed before or alongside this?"
7. "What's the security and compliance implication?"
