# VP of Product

## Role Overview

Owns portfolio coherence, strategy execution, PM team development, and market positioning. Responsible for ensuring the product portfolio delivers on the company's strategic vision while maintaining a high-performing PM team. Operates at the intersection of business strategy, customer needs, and technology capabilities. Success means a product portfolio that captures market opportunity, a PM team that excels, and organizational trust in the product function.

## What They Care About Most

1. **Portfolio coherence and strategy execution** — Individual features must add up to a coherent product strategy. They reject features that don't serve the bigger picture, even popular ones.
2. **Market position and competitive differentiation** — The product must win in the market. They track category dynamics, competitor moves, and analyst perceptions.
3. **PM team growth** — Developing PMs who can think strategically, execute effectively, and influence cross-functionally.
4. **Organizational trust** — Product's credibility depends on shipping what's promised, when it's promised, and demonstrating business impact.
5. **Customer value creation** — Ultimately, the product must solve real customer problems better than alternatives.

## How They Think

Thinks in portfolio: "How do these individual bets add up to a winning market position? Are we investing in the right areas? Are we spreading too thin?" Evaluates proposals by strategic fit — does this advance our position in target segments, or is it a distraction? Balances short-term revenue needs (sales requests) against long-term platform investment. Plans on annual cycles with quarterly recalibration.

## Communication Style

Wants strategic narratives that connect features to market outcomes. Prefers concise executive summaries with clear recommendations. Appreciates when PMs bring options with trade-offs rather than single proposals. Communicates through portfolio reviews, strategy documents, and board-level narratives.

## Common Concerns When Reviewing PM Proposals

- "How does this fit into our product strategy — is this on-strategy or a distraction?"
- "What's the opportunity cost? What are we not doing to invest here?"
- "Are we building for a large enough market, or is this a niche request?"
- "How does this change our competitive position?"
- "What's the measurable outcome — how will we know this succeeded?"

## How to Get Their Buy-In

Frame proposals in strategic context — how this advances market position, not just what it does. Quantify the opportunity with market data or customer evidence. Show awareness of portfolio trade-offs. Propose clear success metrics. Demonstrate cross-functional alignment.

## Red Flags They Watch For

- Features without strategic rationale — "customers asked for it" isn't enough
- PM team overcommitting without ruthless prioritization
- Roadmap that looks like a feature list rather than a strategy
- Product decisions driven by the loudest stakeholder rather than evidence
- Building for a single customer when the market need isn't validated

## Relationship with Product

The VP is the PM team's leader and advocate. They push PMs to think bigger, be more strategic, and communicate more effectively with executives. Friction occurs within the PM team when individual PMs optimize locally rather than thinking portfolio-wide. The VP's job is to ensure the PMs are asking the right questions, not just answering stakeholder requests.

## Key Questions They Will Ask

1. "How does this serve our product strategy and market positioning?"
2. "What's the opportunity size, and how did you validate it?"
3. "What are the portfolio trade-offs — what aren't we doing to invest here?"
4. "What are the measurable success criteria?"
5. "Does this have cross-functional buy-in from engineering, design, and GTM?"
6. "What's the competitive context — how does this change our relative position?"
7. "Is this a one-time feature or a platform investment that compounds over time?"
