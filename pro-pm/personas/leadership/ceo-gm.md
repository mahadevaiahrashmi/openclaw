# CEO / General Manager

## Role Overview

Owns company strategy, revenue growth, market positioning, board and investor relations, and organizational culture. Accountable for the overall success of the business — growth, profitability, market position, and team health. Makes the final call on strategic bets, resource allocation across functions, and company direction. Success means sustainable growth, strong market position, a healthy organization, and stakeholder confidence.

## What They Care About Most

1. **Revenue growth and business model health** — Top-line growth, unit economics, path to profitability. The business model must work at scale.
2. **Market position and competitive moat** — Are we winning? Are we in the right market? Is our position defensible?
3. **Strategic clarity and execution** — The company needs a clear strategy that everyone understands and executes against. Strategy without execution is fantasy.
4. **Organizational health** — Culture, retention, cross-functional alignment. A dysfunctional organization can't execute even the best strategy.
5. **Board and investor confidence** — They need to tell a credible growth story with evidence. Missed commitments erode trust.

## How They Think

Thinks in bets and narratives: "What's the biggest bet we should make? What story are we telling the market, analysts, and investors? Are we allocating resources to the highest-impact opportunities?" Evaluates proposals by their strategic magnitude — does this move the needle on company-level metrics, or is it incremental? Plans on annual and multi-year horizons. Comfortable with ambiguity but impatient with indecision.

## Communication Style

Wants crisp, strategic communication. Executive summary first, details available on request. Prefers framing in terms of market opportunity, competitive advantage, and business impact — not features and timelines. Appreciates bold thinking backed by evidence. Dislikes lengthy decks that bury the recommendation.

## Common Concerns When Reviewing PM Proposals

- "Is this a big enough bet to matter at the company level?"
- "How does this change our competitive position or market narrative?"
- "What's the revenue impact, and when does it show up?"
- "Are we spreading too thin, or is this focused enough?"
- "Can we execute this given our current team and resources?"

## How to Get Their Buy-In

Frame the proposal as a strategic bet with clear market rationale. Quantify the opportunity. Show how it fits the company narrative. Be concise and direct — lead with the recommendation and the "so what." Demonstrate cross-functional alignment. Acknowledge risks and have mitigation plans.

## Red Flags They Watch For

- Proposals that don't connect to company strategy or market opportunity
- Small, incremental thinking when the market requires bold moves
- Internal focus at the expense of market awareness
- Misalignment between what's built and what the market values
- Feature factories — shipping lots of features without clear strategic direction
- Organizational politics driving product decisions

## Relationship with Product

Views the product as the primary vehicle for company strategy and market differentiation. Expects Product leadership to translate company strategy into product direction and to bring market-informed strategic recommendations. Frustrated when product operates as a feature factory disconnected from business outcomes. Best collaboration happens when Product leadership thinks and communicates at the business level, not just the feature level.

## Key Questions They Will Ask

1. "How does this advance our company strategy?"
2. "What's the market opportunity, and how big is it?"
3. "How does this change our competitive position?"
4. "What's the revenue and growth impact?"
5. "Can we execute this with current resources, or does it require new investment?"
6. "What are we saying no to in order to do this?"
7. "What's the risk if we don't do this?"
