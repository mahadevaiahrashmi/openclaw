# Engineering Manager

## Role Overview

Owns team delivery, capacity planning, hiring, and developer morale. Accountable for turning product requirements into shipped software on a predictable cadence. Success means the team delivers high-quality work sustainably — no burnouts, no death marches, no surprise delays. Acts as a shield for the team against organizational chaos while ensuring alignment with business priorities.

## What They Care About Most

1. **Team health and sustainable pace** — Burnout is an engineering manager's worst nightmare. They track velocity not to maximize it but to stabilize it.
2. **Clarity of requirements** — Ambiguous specs create rework, frustration, and missed deadlines. They need to know what "done" looks like before committing.
3. **Predictable delivery** — They've made commitments to their leadership. Scope changes mid-sprint erode trust and credibility.
4. **Developer experience** — Tooling, CI/CD, code review culture, and technical debt all affect how fast and happily the team ships.
5. **Hiring and retention** — Losing a senior engineer costs 6+ months of productivity. They protect team stability fiercely.

## How They Think

Operates on a 1-3 sprint planning horizon for execution, with a quarterly view for capacity and hiring. Looks at velocity trends, cycle time, PR review latency, and incident counts. Makes trade-offs by asking: "What's the cost of this decision on my team's ability to ship next quarter?" Weighs context-switching cost heavily — every new initiative means something else slows down.

## Communication Style

Prefers structured, written communication with clear scope, acceptance criteria, and priority rankings. Wants to know what's changing and why before the team hears about it. Dislikes surprises in all-hands or stakeholder meetings. Appreciates when PMs bring options with trade-offs rather than mandates.

## Common Concerns When Reviewing PM Proposals

- "How does this fit into what we're already committed to this quarter?"
- "Have you accounted for the migration/refactoring this requires?"
- "What can we cut to make room for this?"
- "This timeline assumes zero interruptions — that never happens"
- "Who reviewed the technical feasibility of this estimate?"

## How to Get Their Buy-In

Lead with context on why this matters now (not just why it matters). Show awareness of current team load. Bring a rough scope with clear boundaries — they'll refine the technical approach with you. Offer to cut scope or defer other work to make room. Acknowledge that estimates are uncertain and build in buffer together.

## Red Flags They Watch For

- Scope creep disguised as "small additions"
- Timelines set without engineering input
- Multiple concurrent priority-one items
- Requirements that keep changing after sprint commitment
- Proposals that assume unlimited engineering capacity
- Stakeholder promises made before technical feasibility review

## Relationship with Product

Views the PM as a critical partner when the relationship works — the PM handles the "what and why," the EM handles the "how and when." Friction arises when PMs commit to dates without consulting engineering, when priorities shift without explanation, or when the PM treats engineering as an order-taking function. Collaboration works best with regular 1:1s, shared backlog grooming, and honest conversations about trade-offs.

## Key Questions They Will Ask

1. "What's the priority of this relative to our current commitments?"
2. "What's the smallest version of this we could ship to learn?"
3. "Have we accounted for technical debt and infrastructure work this requires?"
4. "What happens if we miss this timeline — is the date real or aspirational?"
5. "How many teams need to coordinate on this, and who owns the integration points?"
6. "What can we deprioritize to make room?"
7. "Has anyone validated this with the engineers who'd actually build it?"
