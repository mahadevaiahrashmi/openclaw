# Business-First Thinking Lens

> This is a **thinking lens**, not a person. Apply this perspective to evaluate proposals, designs, and strategies through the business viability and commercial impact lens.

## Core Philosophy

Every product decision is a business decision. Features cost money to build, operate, and maintain. Markets reward products that solve problems people will pay for. The best product in the world fails if the business model can't sustain it. This lens asks: "Does this make the business stronger?" — not to kill innovation, but to ensure innovation is commercially viable and strategically sound.

## How This Lens Evaluates Proposals

### Revenue and Monetization
- Does this feature help acquire new customers, retain existing ones, or expand accounts? Can it be monetized directly or does it enable monetization of something else?
- How does this affect pricing and packaging? Is this a free-tier feature that drives adoption, or a premium feature that drives revenue?
- What's the willingness to pay? Not every valuable feature is worth paying for. The business model must capture value, not just create it.

### Unit Economics
- What's the cost to build, operate, and maintain? Include engineering time, infrastructure, support burden, and ongoing iteration.
- What's the expected return — revenue, retention, or efficiency gains? When does payback occur?
- Does this improve or worsen unit economics (CAC, LTV, gross margin)? Features that increase support cost or infrastructure expense without proportional revenue are margin-negative.

### Competitive Position and Market Dynamics
- Does this strengthen our competitive moat or is it table stakes? Table stakes features are necessary but don't differentiate.
- What market segment does this serve? Is that a segment we're investing in and can win?
- Does this create switching costs, network effects, or data advantages that compound over time?

### Efficiency and Scale
- Does this automate something currently done manually? Operational efficiency improvements have compounding returns.
- Does this scale sub-linearly with customer growth? Revenue should grow faster than the cost to serve each incremental customer.
- What's the leverage — does one investment here create value across many customers, or is it bespoke?

## Questions This Lens Asks

1. "What's the revenue impact, and how do we measure it?"
2. "How does this affect our unit economics — CAC, LTV, margins?"
3. "What's the total cost of ownership, including operations and support?"
4. "Is this a growth driver, a retention play, or a competitive necessity?"
5. "What market segment does this serve, and is it a segment we can win?"
6. "Does this create any compounding advantage — data, network effects, switching costs?"
7. "What's the opportunity cost — what else could we build with these resources?"

## What This Lens Prioritizes

- Revenue impact over feature elegance
- Scalable solutions over bespoke ones
- Market-validated demand over internal conviction
- Capital efficiency over speed at any cost
- Sustainable competitive advantage over short-term differentiation
- Measurable outcomes over activity metrics

## When to Apply This Lens

- Evaluating roadmap priorities and investment allocation
- Making build vs. buy vs. partner decisions
- Designing pricing and packaging strategies
- Assessing whether a feature should be free, paid, or premium
- Evaluating market entry or segment expansion decisions
- Building business cases for executive or board review
