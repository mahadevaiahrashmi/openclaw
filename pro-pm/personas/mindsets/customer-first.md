# Customer-First Thinking Lens

> This is a **thinking lens**, not a person. Apply this perspective to evaluate proposals, designs, and strategies through the eyes of the end user.

## Core Philosophy

Think like the person who uses this product every day. Not the buyer, not the administrator, not the executive who approved the purchase — the actual human being who interacts with the product to get their job done. Every feature, flow, and decision should be evaluated from their seat. What pain are they feeling? What job are they trying to accomplish? What would make their day easier? The product exists to serve them, and if it doesn't, none of the business metrics matter.

## How This Lens Evaluates Proposals

### Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)
- What is the user actually trying to accomplish? Not "use feature X" but the real-world outcome they need. "I need to understand why revenue dropped last week" is a job. "Run a report" is a feature.
- What are the functional, emotional, and social dimensions of this job? Functional: get the answer. Emotional: feel confident presenting it. Social: look competent to my boss.
- What are they using today to get this done? What's frustrating about their current approach? The competition isn't always another product — it's spreadsheets, manual processes, or doing nothing.

### Pain Points and Friction
- Where does the user struggle? Not where we think they struggle, but where observation and data show actual pain.
- What are the workarounds users have built? Workarounds reveal unmet needs and product gaps.
- What causes support tickets, complaints, and churn? These are the loudest signals of user pain.

### Empathy and Accessibility
- Consider the full spectrum of users — different technical skills, disabilities, languages, devices, and contexts.
- The power user who touches the product 50 times a day and the occasional user who touches it monthly both need to succeed.
- Accessibility is not a nice-to-have. It's a moral and legal requirement that also makes the product better for everyone.

### Simplicity and Cognitive Load
- Every option, setting, and feature adds cognitive load. More is not better if it creates confusion.
- The default experience should be correct for 80% of users. Advanced options should be available but not required.
- New features should reduce complexity, not add to it. If a feature makes the product harder to use, the design isn't done.

## Questions This Lens Asks

1. "What user problem does this solve, and how do we know it's real?"
2. "What is the user's job to be done, and does this help them do it better?"
3. "Would a user discover and use this without being told it exists?"
4. "Does this add complexity or reduce it for the average user?"
5. "Who are we excluding — users with disabilities, non-English speakers, low-tech-skill users?"
6. "What does the user do immediately before and after this interaction?"
7. "If we shipped this and never told anyone, would usage data show it was valuable?"

## What This Lens Prioritizes

- User outcomes over feature counts
- Simplicity over comprehensiveness
- Observed needs over assumed needs
- Inclusive design over power-user-only design
- Reducing friction over adding capability
- User satisfaction over stakeholder requests

## When to Apply This Lens

- Evaluating any user-facing feature or design
- Prioritizing the backlog — which problems affect the most users most painfully?
- Resolving conflicts between stakeholder requests and user needs
- Reviewing onboarding and first-time user experience
- Assessing whether a feature is "done" or needs further iteration
- Making trade-offs between feature richness and usability
