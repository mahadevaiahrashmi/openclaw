# Nonviolent Communication (NVC)

## What It Is
Nonviolent Communication, developed by Marshall Rosenberg, is a framework for navigating difficult conversations through four steps: Observation (what happened, without judgment), Feeling (how it affects you), Need (the underlying need), and Request (a specific, actionable ask). NVC separates facts from interpretations and focuses on universal human needs rather than blame.

## When to Use
- Difficult conversations with stakeholders, reports, or cross-functional partners
- When conflicts arise from miscommunication or competing priorities
- Addressing team dynamics issues that feel personal
- Any situation where emotions are running high and productive dialogue is at risk

## When NOT to Use
- Performance issues requiring formal HR intervention — NVC is for interpersonal communication
- Urgent situations requiring immediate action — address the urgency first
- When the other person isn't willing to engage in good-faith dialogue

## How to Apply
1. **Observation**: State what you observed — facts, not interpretations. No evaluative language
   - "In the last three sprint reviews, the design updates were not included in the agenda"
   - NOT: "You always forget to include design" (judgment)
2. **Feeling**: Express how this affects you using "I feel..." language
   - "I feel concerned..." / "I feel frustrated..."
   - NOT: "I feel like you don't care" (that's a thought disguised as a feeling)
3. **Need**: Identify the underlying need that isn't being met
   - "...because I need the team to have visibility into design progress to make good planning decisions"
4. **Request**: Make a specific, positive, doable request (not a demand)
   - "Would you be willing to add a 5-minute design update to the sprint review agenda each week?"

## Template
**Observation**: "When I observe/notice [specific, factual observation]..."

**Feeling**: "I feel [emotion — not 'I feel that...']..."

**Need**: "...because I need/value [underlying need]..."

**Request**: "Would you be willing to [specific, actionable request]?"

## Example
**Scenario**: Engineering lead keeps de-scoping PM requirements without discussion

**Without NVC**: "You keep cutting my requirements without asking me. You clearly don't respect the product vision."

**With NVC**:
- **O**: "I noticed that in the last two sprints, three user-facing requirements were removed from the scope without a discussion beforehand"
- **F**: "I feel frustrated and a bit anxious"
- **N**: "...because I need to maintain alignment between what we committed to stakeholders and what we deliver, and I value being part of scope decisions that affect the user experience"
- **R**: "Would you be willing to flag scope changes with me before the sprint starts, so we can evaluate the trade-offs together?"

## Key Pitfalls
- "I feel that you..." — that's a thought/judgment, not a feeling. Stick to emotions: frustrated, worried, disappointed, encouraged
- Making demands disguised as requests — a request can be declined; a demand carries consequences
- Skipping the Need — without it, the request feels arbitrary; the need provides legitimacy
- Using NVC only for conflicts — it's equally useful for expressing appreciation

## Related Frameworks
- **Situation-Behavior-Impact** — simpler feedback format for performance-specific conversations
- **Stakeholder Influence** — NVC supports influencing through empathy and connection
- **Radical Candor** — complementary philosophy: care personally + challenge directly
- **DACI/RACI** — process clarity can prevent many NVC-requiring situations
