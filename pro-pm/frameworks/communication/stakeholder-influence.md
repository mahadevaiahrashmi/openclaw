# Stakeholder Influence

## What It Is
Stakeholder Influence is the art and science of getting things done without formal authority — a core PM skill. Based on Robert Cialdini's six principles of influence (Reciprocity, Commitment, Social Proof, Authority, Liking, Scarcity) and adapted for product management contexts. It's about building the relationships, credibility, and communication skills to align people around your product vision.

## When to Use
- Getting buy-in for product decisions from engineering, design, sales, or executives
- Navigating organizational resistance to product changes
- When you can't mandate — you need to persuade
- Building cross-functional alignment on roadmap priorities

## When NOT to Use
- When you have formal authority and clear mandates — direct communication is simpler
- Manipulative contexts — influence should be used ethically, with genuine mutual benefit
- When the right answer is to listen, not persuade — sometimes stakeholders are right and you need to adapt

## How to Apply
Apply Cialdini's six principles:
1. **Reciprocity**: Give before you ask. Help stakeholders with their goals, share information proactively, do favors. When you need their support, they'll be predisposed to help
2. **Commitment/Consistency**: Get small agreements first. People who've said "yes" to small asks are more likely to say "yes" to larger ones. Start with the principle, then the specifics
3. **Social Proof**: Show that others support this direction. "Three enterprise customers have asked for this" or "The data team has already validated this approach" reduces perceived risk
4. **Authority**: Build credibility through data, expertise, and track record. Cite industry experts, customer research, and your own successful past decisions
5. **Liking**: People say yes to people they like. Invest in relationships before you need them. Be genuinely curious about stakeholders' challenges
6. **Scarcity**: Create urgency through competitive threats, closing windows, or opportunity costs. "If we don't act by Q3, the market window closes"

## Example
**Goal**: Get VP Sales to support self-serve onboarding (she fears it'll reduce sales' role)
- **Reciprocity**: Help her team by building a better sales dashboard they've been requesting
- **Commitment**: Get her to agree that "reducing time-to-value is important" (principle) before proposing self-serve (specific)
- **Social Proof**: Share case studies of competitors whose sales teams grew after launching self-serve (more pipeline, bigger deals)
- **Authority**: Present data showing 60% of trial users want to buy without talking to sales — these are deals sales *can't* capture today
- **Liking**: Regular 1:1 coffee chats, genuine interest in her team's quarterly challenges
- **Scarcity**: "Competitor X launches self-serve next quarter. If we don't act, we lose the differentiation window"

## Key Pitfalls
- Seeing influence as manipulation — ethical influence creates genuine mutual benefit
- Relying on a single principle — use multiple principles in combination
- Influencing without listening — the best influencers first understand others' positions deeply
- Burning political capital on battles that don't matter — save influence for high-stakes decisions
- Not investing in relationships before you need them — influence is built over time, not in the moment

## Related Frameworks
- **Nonviolent Communication** — for difficult conversations during influence efforts
- **RACI/DACI** — formal role clarity that complements informal influence
- **SCQA** — narrative structure for persuasive influence communications
- **Pyramid Principle** — structure your influence arguments clearly
