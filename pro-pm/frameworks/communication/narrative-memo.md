# Narrative Memo (Amazon 6-Pager)

## What It Is
The Narrative Memo is Amazon's preferred communication format: a 6-page (maximum) structured document written in narrative prose — no bullet points. Meetings begin with silent reading of the memo (typically 20 minutes), followed by discussion. The format forces rigorous thinking because narrative prose is harder to write than slides and exposes logical gaps that bullet points hide.

## When to Use
- Major product decisions, strategy proposals, or new initiative pitches
- When the topic requires nuanced analysis that slides can't capture
- Creating a shared understanding before a key decision meeting
- Annual planning documents, business reviews, and post-mortems

## When NOT to Use
- Quick status updates — use a shorter format (Pyramid Principle, SCQA)
- Technical specifications — use a structured spec format
- When the audience can't dedicate 20+ minutes to reading

## How to Apply
1. **Write in narrative prose**: Complete sentences and paragraphs, not bullets. This forces clear thinking
2. **Structure the document**:
   - **Introduction**: Context, why this matters now (SCQA format works well here)
   - **Problem/Opportunity**: Detailed analysis of the current state and the gap
   - **Proposed approach**: What you recommend and how it works
   - **Key considerations**: Trade-offs, risks, alternatives considered, cost/resource implications
   - **Metrics and milestones**: How you'll measure success and key checkpoints
   - **Appendix**: Supporting data, detailed analysis (doesn't count toward the 6-page limit)
3. **Edit ruthlessly**: The 6-page limit forces prioritization. Every sentence must earn its place
4. **Include data**: Support claims with data, not opinions. Include charts and tables inline
5. **Meeting protocol**: Distribute at the start of the meeting. Silent reading. Then discuss — starting with questions, not presentations

## Template
**[Title of Proposal]**

**1. Introduction** (½ page): Why are we here? What's the context? What are we deciding?

**2. The Problem/Opportunity** (1 page): What's happening? Data-supported analysis of the current state

**3. Proposed Approach** (2 pages): What do we recommend? How will it work? Why this approach over alternatives?

**4. Key Considerations** (1 page): Trade-offs, risks, resource requirements, dependencies, alternatives rejected

**5. Metrics & Milestones** (½ page): Success criteria, timeline, checkpoints

**6. Ask** (½ page): What do you need from this audience? Decision, resources, alignment?

**Appendix**: [Supporting data — not counted in 6 pages]

## Example
**Memo title**: "Proposal: Self-Serve Enterprise Tier"
- **Introduction**: Enterprise revenue is 60% of ARR but 100% sales-assisted. Self-serve enterprise could unlock $8M ARR with lower CAC
- **Problem**: Enterprise sales cycle averages 90 days with $12K CAC. 40% of enterprise evaluators want to buy without talking to sales but can't
- **Proposal**: Launch self-serve enterprise tier with annual billing, SSO, and audit logs. Price at $1,200/user/year. Support with automated onboarding and in-app contract signing
- **Key Considerations**: Risk of cannibalizing existing sales pipeline (mitigated by $50K+ deals remaining sales-assisted). Legal review needed for self-serve contracts
- **Metrics**: Self-serve enterprise ARR, enterprise CAC, sales pipeline impact
- **Ask**: Approve 2-engineer investment for Q3; legal review of self-serve contract by end of month

## Key Pitfalls
- Using bullets disguised as prose — write real paragraphs with logical flow
- No data — "we believe" is weak; "data shows" is strong
- Too much detail in the body — move detailed analysis to appendices
- Skipping the silent reading in meetings — presenting defeats the purpose

## Related Frameworks
- **Working Backwards** — Amazon's PR/FAQ format for new product concepts
- **Pyramid Principle** — structure each section with answer-first logic
- **SCQA** — useful structure for the introduction section
- **SPADE** — for documenting decisions made based on the memo
