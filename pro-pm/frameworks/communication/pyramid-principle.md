# Pyramid Principle

## What It Is
The Pyramid Principle, created by Barbara Minto at McKinsey, is a communication framework that structures arguments top-down: lead with the answer, then provide supporting arguments, then details. Groups of arguments must be MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive). It's the standard for executive communication in consulting and business.

## When to Use
- Executive summaries, stakeholder updates, and product reviews
- Any written communication where the reader is busy and needs the key point first
- Structuring PRDs, strategy docs, and decision recommendations
- When feedback on your communication is "too long" or "what's the point?"

## When NOT to Use
- Creative writing or storytelling where suspense matters
- Sensitive communications where leading with the conclusion would be jarring
- Exploratory discussions where the conclusion isn't yet known

## How to Apply
1. **Start with the answer**: State your main point/recommendation in the first sentence
2. **Group supporting arguments**: 2–4 arguments that support your main point. Each should be independent (Mutually Exclusive) and together they should be complete (Collectively Exhaustive)
3. **Support each argument**: Provide data, examples, or sub-arguments under each
4. **MECE check**: Do your arguments overlap (not mutually exclusive)? Are there gaps (not collectively exhaustive)?
5. **Order arguments**: By importance (strongest first), by chronology, or by structure (if logical order exists)

## Template
```
MAIN POINT: [Answer/recommendation]
├── Argument 1: [Supporting reason]
│   ├── Evidence/data
│   └── Evidence/data
├── Argument 2: [Supporting reason]
│   ├── Evidence/data
│   └── Evidence/data
└── Argument 3: [Supporting reason]
    ├── Evidence/data
    └── Evidence/data
```

## Example
**Bad (bottom-up)**: "We looked at user feedback, analyzed the data, talked to the sales team, reviewed competitor moves, and after considering all options, we think we should invest in self-serve onboarding."

**Good (pyramid)**: "We should invest in self-serve onboarding this quarter. Three reasons: (1) 65% of trial users drop off before completing setup — our biggest conversion leak. (2) Sales team spends 40% of their time on onboarding calls that could be automated — freeing capacity for enterprise deals. (3) Competitors launched self-serve in Q2 and their trial conversion doubled."

## Key Pitfalls
- Burying the lead — if the reader has to scroll to find your point, you've lost them
- Arguments that aren't MECE — overlapping points confuse; gaps undermine credibility
- Too many levels — 2–3 levels of depth is enough for most communications
- Applying pyramid to every conversation — casual discussions don't need formal structure

## Related Frameworks
- **SCQA** — narrative storytelling structure that complements the Pyramid for setups
- **Narrative Memo** — Amazon's long-form application of structured communication
- **SPADE** — decision communication framework with structured explanation
- **Situation-Behavior-Impact** — feedback-specific communication structure
