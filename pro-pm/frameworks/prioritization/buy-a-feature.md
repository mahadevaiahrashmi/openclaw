# Buy a Feature

## What It Is
Buy a Feature is a collaborative prioritization game developed by Luke Hohmann (Innovation Games). Participants receive a fixed budget of fake money and "purchase" the features they value most. Some features are priced high enough to require pooling money, forcing negotiation and revealing collective priorities.

## When to Use
- Involving customers or stakeholders directly in prioritization
- Advisory boards, customer summits, or stakeholder workshops
- When you want to surface hidden preferences and force trade-off conversations
- Validating willingness-to-pay signals in a low-stakes environment

## When NOT to Use
- Internal backlog grooming where data-driven methods (RICE) are more appropriate
- When participants don't represent your actual user base
- Highly technical decisions where participants lack context to evaluate options
- Remote async work — this works best as a live, facilitated exercise

## How to Apply
1. **Select features** (15–25 is ideal). Mix sizes and types
2. **Price features** based on estimated effort or strategic value. Ensure some require collaboration to buy
3. **Set budgets**: Give each participant enough to buy ~3–5 mid-range features (roughly 1/3 of total feature cost across all features)
4. **Run the game** (30–60 minutes):
   - Participants review features and prices
   - They allocate money independently or negotiate to pool resources
   - Facilitator captures reasoning and discussions
5. **Tally results**: Count total spend per feature
6. **Debrief**: Discuss why certain features attracted pooled investment. Capture qualitative insights

## Example
**Context**: B2B SaaS advisory board (8 enterprise customers, $100 each)
- API rate limit increase ($40): 5 buyers pooled → $200 ← top priority
- Audit log export ($25): 6 buyers → $150
- Custom branding ($30): 2 buyers → $60
- Mobile app ($50): 1 buyer → $50
- AI assistant ($35): 3 buyers → $105

→ API limits and audit logs are clear priorities. Mobile app had only one champion despite high individual spend.

## Key Pitfalls
- Pricing too low — if everyone can buy everything, no trade-offs happen
- Letting the loudest voice dominate group purchasing decisions
- Treating results as statistically significant — this is qualitative research, not a survey
- Not capturing the *why* behind purchases — the discussion is as valuable as the numbers
