# Design Thinking

## What It Is
Design Thinking is a human-centered problem-solving methodology developed at IDEO and Stanford d.school. It follows five phases — Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test — that are iterative, not linear. It emphasizes deep understanding of user needs before generating solutions, and rapid prototyping to learn quickly.

## When to Use
- Tackling ambiguous, complex problems where the right solution isn't obvious
- Redesigning user experiences or workflows
- Innovating beyond incremental improvements
- Cross-functional workshops to align teams around user needs

## When NOT to Use
- Well-defined problems with known solutions — just build them
- When speed to market matters more than design quality — use lean approaches
- Optimizing existing flows with quantitative data — use A/B testing and analytics

## How to Apply
1. **Empathize**: Immerse yourself in the user's world. Interview users, observe behavior, use contextual inquiry. Build genuine understanding of their experiences, motivations, and frustrations
2. **Define**: Synthesize empathy research into a clear problem statement. Use "How Might We" (HMW) questions. Create personas or archetypes. Articulate the specific user need
3. **Ideate**: Generate a wide range of possible solutions. Diverge before converging. Use brainstorming, Crazy 8s, "worst possible idea." Defer judgment during ideation
4. **Prototype**: Build quick, low-fidelity representations of ideas. Paper prototypes, wireframes, role-playing, Wizard of Oz. The goal is to learn, not to polish
5. **Test**: Put prototypes in front of real users. Observe, don't lead. Gather feedback. Iterate — return to any previous phase as needed

## Example
**Challenge**: Improve the expense reporting experience for sales reps
- **Empathize**: Shadowed 5 sales reps; discovered they photograph receipts but lose them before submitting
- **Define**: "Sales reps need to capture and submit expenses in the moment because they forget and lose receipts within 24 hours"
- **Ideate**: Auto-scan from camera, email-forward receipts, GPS-triggered reminders, shared team expense pools
- **Prototype**: Paper prototype of a "snap and submit" mobile flow
- **Test**: 8 reps used the prototype for a week — 6 preferred it to current process; 2 wanted manual categorization

## Key Pitfalls
- Skipping Empathize and jumping to Ideate — the most common mistake; solutions without understanding fail
- Treating the five phases as linear — iteration is the point; go back when you learn something new
- Prototyping too polished — high-fidelity prototypes bias testers toward surface reactions
- Running one workshop and calling it "Design Thinking" — it's a continuous practice, not an event

## Related Frameworks
- **Lean Startup** — complementary: design thinking for discovery, lean for validation
- **Jobs to Be Done** — provides structure for the Empathize and Define phases
- **Empathy Map** — specific tool for the Empathize phase
- **Dual-Track Agile** — integrates design thinking (discovery) with delivery
