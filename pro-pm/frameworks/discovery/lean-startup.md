# Lean Startup

## What It Is
The Lean Startup, developed by Eric Ries (2011), is a methodology for building products under conditions of extreme uncertainty. Its core loop — Build-Measure-Learn — emphasizes creating Minimum Viable Products (MVPs) to test hypotheses, measuring results with actionable metrics, and making data-informed decisions to pivot or persevere. It applies scientific method to entrepreneurship.

## When to Use
- New products or features with significant uncertainty about customer need or viability
- Startup environments where resources are limited and speed of learning matters
- Internal ventures or innovation projects exploring new markets
- When you need to validate demand before investing in full development

## When NOT to Use
- Mature products optimizing known metrics — use growth/analytics frameworks
- Regulated industries where MVPs may violate compliance requirements
- When the problem and solution are well-understood — just execute
- Infrastructure or platform work where "minimum viable" doesn't apply

## How to Apply
1. **Formulate hypotheses**: State your value hypothesis (will users find it valuable?) and growth hypothesis (how will users discover it?)
2. **Build an MVP**: The smallest thing that tests your riskiest hypothesis. Types: landing page, Wizard of Oz, concierge, single-feature prototype, video demo
3. **Define actionable metrics**: Metrics that inform decisions (conversion rate, retention) vs. vanity metrics (total signups, page views)
4. **Measure**: Run the experiment and collect data
5. **Learn**: Did the hypothesis hold? What did you learn?
6. **Decide**: Pivot (change strategy) or Persevere (continue and scale)
7. **Repeat**: Each cycle should be faster than the last

## Example
**Hypothesis**: Freelance designers will pay $20/month for an AI portfolio generator
- **MVP**: Landing page with pricing, "Sign up for early access" button
- **Actionable Metric**: Email signup conversion rate (target: >5% of visitors)
- **Result**: 8.2% conversion over 2 weeks (1,200 visitors, 98 signups)
- **Learning**: Demand signal is strong. Next: test willingness to pay with a Stripe checkout
- **Decision**: Persevere — build the concierge MVP for the first 20 signups

## Key Pitfalls
- Building too much in the MVP — it's "minimum" for a reason; test one hypothesis at a time
- Using vanity metrics — total signups doesn't tell you if users find value
- Pivoting too early (not enough data) or too late (sunk cost fallacy)
- Treating Build-Measure-Learn as waterfall — it's a rapid, iterative loop

## Related Frameworks
- **Lean Canvas** — one-page business model designed for lean startup methodology
- **Assumption Mapping** — identifies which hypotheses to test first
- **Design Thinking** — complements lean startup with deeper user empathy
- **Continuous Discovery** — Teresa Torres' structured approach to ongoing learning
