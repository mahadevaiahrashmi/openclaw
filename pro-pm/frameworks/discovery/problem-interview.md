# Problem Interview

## What It Is
The Problem Interview is a structured approach to validating whether a problem exists, is painful enough to solve, and how customers currently cope. Rooted in Lean Startup and Customer Development, it focuses on understanding the customer's world *before* proposing solutions. The key discipline: you're testing the problem, not pitching your product.

## When to Use
- Early-stage validation before building anything
- When you suspect a problem exists but don't have evidence
- Continuous discovery interviews to explore new opportunity areas
- When pivoting — understanding if the new problem space is real

## When NOT to Use
- When the problem is well-validated and you need to test solutions — use solution interviews
- Usability testing of existing features — different methodology
- When you need quantitative validation at scale — use surveys after qualitative validation

## How to Apply
1. **Prepare**: Define the top 3 problems you believe exist. Identify your target customer segment
2. **Recruit**: 10–15 people from your target segment. Recruit based on behavior, not demographics
3. **Interview structure** (30–40 minutes):
   - **Welcome** (2 min): Set context. "I'm exploring a problem space, not selling anything"
   - **Collect demographics** (2 min): Confirm they're in your target segment
   - **Tell a story** (3 min): Describe the problem context. "Many people tell me they struggle with..."
   - **Problem ranking** (5 min): Present your top 3 problems. Ask them to rank by severity
   - **Explore each problem** (15 min): For the top-ranked problem, dig deep. "Tell me about the last time..." "What did you do?" "What happened next?"
   - **Current solutions** (5 min): How do they solve this today? What do they use? What do they spend?
   - **Wrap up** (3 min): Ask for referrals. Thank them
4. **Synthesize**: After 5 interviews, look for patterns. After 10, expect convergence
5. **Decide**: Is the problem real, painful, and currently underserved?

## Example
**Context**: Exploring if engineering managers struggle with tracking team health
- Hypothesis: EMs can't identify burnout until it's too late
- Interviewee: VP Eng at 200-person company
- Top Problem: "I don't know who's overloaded until they're already burned out or leave"
- Current Solution: 1:1s (inconsistent), gut feeling, Slack activity monitoring (feels invasive)
- Key Quote: "By the time someone tells me they're struggling, they've already decided to leave"
- Pattern (after 8 interviews): 7/8 confirmed this; 6/8 had no systematic approach

## Key Pitfalls
- Pitching your solution during the interview — you're there to listen, not sell
- Asking "Would you use X?" — hypothetical questions get hypothetical answers
- Interviewing friends/colleagues who'll tell you what you want to hear
- Stopping after 3 interviews — do at least 10 to find reliable patterns
- Not asking about current behavior — what people *do* matters more than what they *say*

## Related Frameworks
- **Customer Development** — Steve Blank's broader methodology that includes problem interviews
- **Continuous Discovery** — integrates problem interviews into a weekly habit
- **Jobs to Be Done** — provides the lens for understanding the problem context
- **Lean Startup** — the methodology that problem interviews support
