# Sean Ellis Test

## What It Is
The Sean Ellis Test is a survey-based method for measuring product-market fit quantitatively. The core question: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" If 40%+ of users answer "Very disappointed," you've likely achieved PMF. Beyond the threshold, the test includes follow-up analysis to understand who your most passionate users are, what they value, and how to find more of them.

## When to Use
- Quantitatively measuring product-market fit at any stage
- Tracking PMF progress over time as you iterate
- Identifying your ideal customer segment (where PMF is strongest)
- Making go/no-go decisions on scaling investments

## When NOT to Use
- Products with no active users yet
- As the sole measure — combine with retention curves and engagement data
- When you have fewer than 30 respondents (insufficient sample)

## How to Apply
1. **Survey engaged users**: Target users who have used the core product at least 2x in the last 2 weeks
2. **Core question**: "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?"
   - Very disappointed / Somewhat disappointed / Not disappointed / N/A
3. **Follow-up questions** (for deeper analysis):
   - "What type of people do you think would most benefit from [product]?" → Reveals your real ICP
   - "What is the main benefit you receive?" → Reveals your actual value proposition
   - "How can we improve [product] for you?" → Reveals the roadmap
4. **Calculate the score**: % Very Disappointed
5. **Analyze by segment**: Break down by user type, company size, use case. Find where PMF is strongest
6. **Apply the Superhuman playbook**: If below 40%, focus on the "Very Disappointed" segment and build for them

## Example
**Product**: Developer documentation platform (surveyed 180 active users)
- Overall PMF Score: 35% (below threshold)
- **Segment: Open-source projects**: 52% Very Disappointed → **PMF exists here**
- **Segment: Enterprise internal docs**: 22% Very Disappointed → No PMF
- Main benefit (from Very Disappointed): "Makes our docs beautiful and keeps them in sync with code"
- **Action**: Focus on open-source segment. Build features they want. Don't dilute by chasing enterprise yet

## Key Pitfalls
- Surveying the wrong users (all signups instead of engaged users) — inflates "Not Disappointed"
- Not segmenting — your overall score may be below 40% while a specific segment is above it
- Treating 40% as binary truth — 38% is close; 15% is clearly not. It's a spectrum
- Running the survey once and never again — track quarterly to measure improvement
- Ignoring the open-ended responses — "How can we improve?" is a roadmap goldmine
