# Minimum Lovable Product (MLP)

## What It Is
The Minimum Lovable Product is an evolution of the MVP concept. While an MVP tests whether users will *use* a product (viability), an MLP tests whether users will *love* it (desirability). An MLP is the smallest possible product that creates an emotional response — delight, not just tolerance. The goal: users who actively recommend the product, not users who merely don't uninstall it.

## When to Use
- When your MVP validated demand but users are lukewarm — they use it but don't love it
- Launching in competitive markets where "good enough" won't differentiate
- Products where word-of-mouth and organic growth are critical (no big marketing budget)
- When early user feedback is "it works, but..." — the "but" is what MLP addresses

## When NOT to Use
- Very early-stage validation where you need to test whether anyone cares — use MVP first
- B2B enterprise where lovability matters less than compliance, integration, and ROI
- Internal tools where adoption is mandated — focus on usability, not delight

## How to Apply
1. **Define "lovable"**: What would make users actively recommend this? Not just "works" but "delights"
2. **Identify the core experience**: What single workflow or moment should feel magical? Focus all polish there
3. **Apply the Kano lens**: Include all Must-haves, key Performance features, and at least one Delighter
4. **Design for emotion**: Speed, aesthetics, thoughtful defaults, delightful micro-interactions, personality
5. **Cut ruthlessly everything else**: MLP is still "minimum" — but the minimum is defined by love, not viability
6. **Measure love**: NPS > 50, Sean Ellis > 40%, organic referral rate, unsolicited positive feedback, social sharing

## Template
| Element | MVP Version | MLP Version |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Core experience | Works | Delights |
| Edge cases | Handled minimally | Handled gracefully |
| Onboarding | Functional | Guided and encouraging |
| Design quality | Acceptable | Polished and distinctive |
| Performance | Adequate | Fast and responsive |
| Personality | Generic | Distinctive voice/brand |

**MLP Criteria**:
- [ ] Core experience creates a "wow" moment
- [ ] Users voluntarily recommend to others
- [ ] Sean Ellis score > 40% among target segment
- [ ] Distinctive from alternatives — not just "another X"

## Example
**Product**: Note-taking app
- **MVP**: Create notes, organize in folders, search, sync across devices. Functional but forgettable
- **MLP additions**: Beautiful typography that makes writing feel good. Instant, zero-lag typing. Smart linking between notes that surfaces connections you didn't know existed. Satisfying animations on save and organize
- **What was cut**: Templates (nice but not lovable), collaboration (separate product phase), AI summarization (not core to the writing experience)
- **Result**: Users describe it as "the first note app that makes me *want* to write" — organic sharing drives 40% of new signups

## Key Pitfalls
- Confusing "lovable" with "feature-rich" — MLP is about depth of experience, not breadth of features
- Over-investing in polish before validating the core concept — validate with MVP first, then MLP
- Defining "lovable" based on what the team loves, not what users love — test with real users
- Treating MLP as "MVP plus marketing" — lovability comes from product experience, not messaging
- Scope creep disguised as "making it lovable" — stay minimum; just make the minimum lovable

## Related Frameworks
- **Lean Startup** — MVP is for validation; MLP is for differentiation and PMF
- **Kano Model** — identifies must-haves vs. delighters that make a product lovable
- **Sean Ellis Test** — measures whether you've achieved the love that MLP targets
- **Hook Model** — designing the habit loops that make users return and love the product
