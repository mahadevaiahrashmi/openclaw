# Ansoff Matrix

## What It Is
The Ansoff Matrix (Igor Ansoff, 1957) is a 2×2 strategic planning tool that maps growth options along two dimensions: markets (existing vs. new) and products (existing vs. new). It produces four growth strategies with increasing risk, helping teams choose and communicate their growth direction.

## When to Use
- Annual/quarterly strategic planning to define growth direction
- Evaluating whether to deepen in current markets or expand to new ones
- Portfolio decisions about where to invest R&D and go-to-market resources
- Communicating growth strategy to leadership and investors

## When NOT to Use
- Feature-level prioritization — use RICE or value-effort matrix
- When you need tactical execution plans — Ansoff defines direction, not how to get there
- Very early-stage startups still finding PMF — focus on one quadrant first

## How to Apply
1. **Map your current position**: Which markets do you serve? Which products do you offer?
2. **Evaluate each quadrant**:
   - **Market Penetration** (existing product, existing market): Grow share. Lowest risk. Tactics: pricing, marketing, retention, upsell
   - **Product Development** (new product, existing market): Build new capabilities for current customers. Medium risk
   - **Market Development** (existing product, new market): Expand geographically, new segments, new channels. Medium risk
   - **Diversification** (new product, new market): Highest risk. Related diversification (adjacent) or unrelated (conglomerate)
3. **Assess risk and resource requirements** for each option
4. **Choose primary and secondary** growth strategies
5. **Allocate resources** accordingly

## Template
```
                EXISTING PRODUCTS          NEW PRODUCTS
EXISTING    ┌─────────────────────┬─────────────────────┐
MARKETS     │ Market Penetration  │ Product Development  │
            │ Risk: Low           │ Risk: Medium         │
            │ Strategy:           │ Strategy:            │
            ├─────────────────────┼─────────────────────┤
NEW         │ Market Development  │ Diversification      │
MARKETS     │ Risk: Medium        │ Risk: High           │
            │ Strategy:           │ Strategy:            │
            └─────────────────────┴─────────────────────┘
```

## Example
**Company**: B2B project management tool (current: SMB in North America)
- **Market Penetration**: Increase SMB conversion rate, reduce churn, upsell premium tiers
- **Product Development**: Add resource planning and time tracking for existing SMB customers
- **Market Development**: Launch in EMEA; target mid-market with existing product
- **Diversification**: Build a CRM product for enterprise (highest risk — avoid unless well-resourced)

→ Primary: Market Penetration (80% of investment). Secondary: Market Development into EMEA (20%).

## Key Pitfalls
- Pursuing all four quadrants simultaneously — spread too thin
- Underestimating diversification risk — most diversification efforts fail
- Confusing "new product" with "new feature" — Ansoff means meaningfully new offerings
- Ignoring the diagonal — Product Development + Market Development simultaneously is nearly as risky as Diversification

## Related Frameworks
- **Three Horizons** — time-based portfolio allocation that complements Ansoff
- **BCG Matrix** — portfolio management for existing products/markets
- **Blue Ocean** — creating entirely new market space (extreme diversification)
- **Lean Canvas** — for validating new quadrant entries before full investment
