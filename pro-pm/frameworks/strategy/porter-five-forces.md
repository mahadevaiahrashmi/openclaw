# Porter's Five Forces

## What It Is
Porter's Five Forces, developed by Michael Porter at Harvard Business School (1979), is a framework for analyzing competitive intensity and attractiveness of an industry. It examines five structural forces that shape competition and profitability: rivalry, new entrants, substitutes, buyer power, and supplier power.

## When to Use
- Evaluating whether to enter a new market or industry
- Understanding competitive dynamics before product strategy decisions
- Assessing why margins are compressed or where pricing power exists
- Annual strategic planning and market positioning reviews

## When NOT to Use
- Day-to-day feature prioritization — use RICE or value-effort matrix
- Fast-moving tech markets where industry boundaries are blurry — pair with disruption theory
- Internal product decisions unrelated to competitive dynamics

## How to Apply
1. **Competitive Rivalry**: How intense is competition among existing players? Consider number of competitors, industry growth rate, product differentiation, switching costs, exit barriers
2. **Threat of New Entrants**: How easy is it for new competitors to enter? Consider capital requirements, economies of scale, brand loyalty, regulatory barriers, network effects
3. **Threat of Substitutes**: Can customers solve this problem differently? Consider price-performance of alternatives, switching costs, buyer propensity to substitute
4. **Bargaining Power of Buyers**: Can customers pressure you on price/features? Consider buyer concentration, volume, switching costs, information availability
5. **Bargaining Power of Suppliers**: Can suppliers dictate terms? Consider supplier concentration, differentiation, switching costs, threat of forward integration
6. **Rate each force** (Low / Medium / High) and identify strategic implications

## Example
**Industry**: Cloud infrastructure (IaaS)
- **Rivalry**: High — AWS, Azure, GCP in intense price/feature competition
- **New Entrants**: Low — massive capital and scale requirements
- **Substitutes**: Medium — on-prem, edge computing, specialized PaaS
- **Buyer Power**: Medium — switching costs are high but multi-cloud reduces lock-in
- **Supplier Power**: Low — commodity hardware, open-source software

→ Attractive for incumbents, hostile for new entrants. Compete on ecosystem, not price alone.

## Key Pitfalls
- Static analysis in dynamic markets — forces shift; reassess regularly
- Treating forces as independent — they interact (e.g., high buyer power attracts substitutes)
- Ignoring complements — Porter's original model doesn't account for complementary products
- Using it for individual product decisions when it's designed for industry-level analysis

## Related Frameworks
- **SWOT** — internal/external analysis that complements Five Forces
- **Blue Ocean** — escaping competitive forces entirely
- **PESTEL** — macro-environmental factors affecting the industry
- **Wardley Mapping** — maps value chain evolution that Five Forces doesn't capture
