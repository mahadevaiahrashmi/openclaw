# BCG Growth-Share Matrix

## What It Is
The BCG Matrix, created by Boston Consulting Group (Bruce Henderson, 1970), classifies business units or products into four categories based on market growth rate and relative market share. It guides resource allocation across a portfolio: invest in Stars, harvest Cash Cows, evaluate Question Marks, and divest Dogs.

## When to Use
- Portfolio management across multiple products or business units
- Resource allocation decisions — where to invest, maintain, or sunset
- Strategic reviews with leadership to visualize the product portfolio
- Acquisition or divestiture analysis

## When NOT to Use
- Single-product companies — you need a portfolio to classify
- Feature prioritization within a product — use RICE or value-effort matrix
- Fast-changing markets where market share is volatile — data may be unreliable

## How to Apply
1. **Define the units**: Products, product lines, or business units
2. **Assess market growth rate** (Y-axis): High-growth (>10%/year) vs. low-growth
3. **Assess relative market share** (X-axis): Your share vs. the largest competitor
4. **Plot each unit** on the 2×2 matrix:
   - **Stars** (high growth, high share): Market leaders in growing markets. Invest heavily
   - **Cash Cows** (low growth, high share): Mature, profitable. Harvest for cash
   - **Question Marks** (high growth, low share): High potential, high risk. Invest selectively or divest
   - **Dogs** (low growth, low share): Low potential. Divest or wind down
5. **Define strategy** for each quadrant
6. **Rebalance portfolio** — use Cash Cow profits to fund Stars and select Question Marks

## Template
```
         HIGH MARKET GROWTH
              │
  Question    │    Stars
  Marks       │    (Invest)
  (Evaluate)  │
              │
LOW SHARE ────┼──── HIGH SHARE
              │
  Dogs        │    Cash Cows
  (Divest)    │    (Harvest)
              │
         LOW MARKET GROWTH
```

|-------------|--------------|-------------------|----------|----------|
|             |              |                   |          |          |

## Example
**Company**: Multi-product SaaS company
- **Star**: AI analytics product (market growing 40%/year, #2 market position) → Invest in sales and R&D
- **Cash Cow**: Legacy reporting tool (market flat, #1 position) → Maintain, minimize investment
- **Question Mark**: New collaboration module (market growing 25%, #5 position) → Invest or kill by Q4
- **Dog**: On-premise deployment option (shrinking market, low share) → Sunset over 12 months

## Key Pitfalls
- Oversimplifying — real products don't fit neatly into four boxes
- Killing Dogs prematurely — some "Dogs" are strategic (protect other products, regulatory requirement)
- Ignoring synergies between products in different quadrants
- Using only two dimensions — other factors (strategic importance, tech debt) matter too

## Related Frameworks
- **Three Horizons** — time-based portfolio allocation complementing BCG's market-based view
- **Ansoff Matrix** — growth strategy for products in each BCG quadrant
- **GE-McKinsey Matrix** — more nuanced 3×3 portfolio matrix with multiple factors
- **Value Chain Analysis** — internal view of where value is created per product
