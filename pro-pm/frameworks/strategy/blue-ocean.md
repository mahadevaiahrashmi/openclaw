# Blue Ocean Strategy

## What It Is
Blue Ocean Strategy, developed by W. Chan Kim and Renée Mauborgne (INSEAD, 2005), argues that companies should stop competing in crowded "red ocean" markets and instead create uncontested "blue ocean" market space. The core tool is the Four Actions Framework (Eliminate, Reduce, Raise, Create) applied to a Strategy Canvas.

## When to Use
- When your market is commoditized and competing on features/price is a losing game
- Exploring new market creation or radical product differentiation
- Strategic planning offsites to challenge assumptions about "table stakes"
- When you want to redefine the value curve rather than optimize within it

## When NOT to Use
- Incremental product improvement in an established category — use Kano or JTBD
- When your product hasn't achieved PMF yet — find fit first, then differentiate
- Short-term tactical decisions — Blue Ocean is a long-term strategic tool

## How to Apply
1. **Draw the Strategy Canvas**: X-axis = key competing factors in your industry. Y-axis = offering level (high/low). Plot your product and competitors
2. **Apply the Four Actions Framework**:
   - **Eliminate**: Which factors that the industry competes on should be eliminated entirely?
   - **Reduce**: Which factors should be reduced well below the industry standard?
   - **Raise**: Which factors should be raised well above the industry standard?
   - **Create**: Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered?
3. **Draw the new value curve** on the Strategy Canvas
4. **Validate** that the new curve creates a leap in value for buyers while reducing cost structure
5. **Test three characteristics**: Focus (not trying to do everything), Divergence (different from competitors), Compelling tagline (easy to communicate)

## Example
**Product**: Cirque du Soleil (classic Blue Ocean case)
- **Eliminate**: Star performers, animal shows, aisle concessions, multiple rings
- **Reduce**: Fun and humor, thrill and danger
- **Raise**: Unique venue, artistic music and dance
- **Create**: Theme, refined environment, multiple productions, artistic story

→ Created a new category: theatrical circus experience at theater-level pricing.

## Key Pitfalls
- Thinking "Blue Ocean" means "no competition" — it means creating new demand, not ignoring reality
- Eliminating factors customers actually need — validate with customer research
- Creating without reducing — you need to fund creation by reducing investment elsewhere
- Assuming blue oceans last forever — competitors will follow; plan for defending your position

## Related Frameworks
- **Porter's Five Forces** — the "red ocean" analysis that Blue Ocean challenges
- **Disruption Theory** — related concept of creating new market space from below
- **Value Proposition Canvas** — detailed mapping of customer value creation
- **Wardley Mapping** — maps evolution that can reveal blue ocean opportunities
