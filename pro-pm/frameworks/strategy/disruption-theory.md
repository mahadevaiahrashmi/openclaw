# Disruption Theory

## What It Is
Disruption Theory, developed by Clayton Christensen (The Innovator's Dilemma, 1997), explains how smaller companies with fewer resources can successfully challenge established incumbents. Disruptive innovations start in low-end or new-market footholds and gradually move upmarket, eventually displacing incumbents who over-serve mainstream customers.

## When to Use
- Evaluating whether a competitor or startup poses a disruptive threat
- Designing a market entry strategy against established players
- Understanding why incumbents keep losing to seemingly inferior products
- Strategic planning around innovation portfolio (sustaining vs. disruptive investments)

## When NOT to Use
- All innovation isn't disruption — most innovation is sustaining (better products for existing customers)
- When you need execution frameworks — disruption theory explains *why*, not *how*
- As justification for building inferior products — disruption requires a specific market dynamic

## How to Apply
1. **Identify the type of disruption**:
   - **Low-End Disruption**: Enter at the bottom of an existing market with a "good enough" product at a lower price. Incumbents cede low-margin customers willingly
   - **New-Market Disruption**: Target non-consumers — people who couldn't access the existing solution. Create a new market entirely
2. **Assess the incumbent's trajectory**: Are they over-serving mainstream customers? (Adding features nobody needs at prices many can't afford?)
3. **Identify the "good enough" threshold**: At what point does a simpler, cheaper product meet most customers' needs?
4. **Map the disruption path**: Start in foothold → improve over time → move upmarket → displace incumbents
5. **Evaluate your position**: Are you the disruptor or the incumbent? Act accordingly

## Example
**Case**: Notion disrupting enterprise project management (Jira/Asana)
- Type: Low-end disruption (simpler, cheaper) + New-market (non-PM users who need lightweight planning)
- Incumbents over-serving: Jira's complexity exceeds most teams' needs
- Good enough: Basic task tracking + docs + wiki in one tool
- Foothold: Startups and small teams who find Jira too complex
- Upmarket path: Adding databases, automations, enterprise features

## Key Pitfalls
- Labeling everything "disruptive" — most innovations are sustaining, and that's fine
- Assuming disruption is inevitable — incumbents can respond if they recognize the pattern early
- Ignoring that disruptive products must improve over time — starting simple is the strategy, not the endpoint
- Applying disruption theory to markets without clear over-serving dynamics

## Related Frameworks

- **Crossing the Chasm** — adoption challenges disruptors face when moving upmarket
