# Business Model Canvas

## What It Is
The Business Model Canvas (BMC), created by Alexander Osterwalder and Yves Pigneur (Business Model Generation, 2010), describes a business model through nine building blocks on a single page. It provides a shared language for describing, visualizing, assessing, and changing business models.

## When to Use
- Designing a new business model for a product or company
- Assessing and comparing business models (yours vs. competitors)
- Strategic pivots — visualizing what changes when you shift strategy
- Investor and stakeholder communication — one-page strategic overview

## When NOT to Use
- Startup hypotheses — use Lean Canvas (designed for uncertainty)
- Detailed financial modeling — BMC is a strategic view, not a financial one
- Feature-level planning — too high-level for product backlog decisions

## How to Apply
1. Fill in the nine blocks, starting from the right (customer side):
   - **Customer Segments**: Who are your customers? Which segments do you serve?
   - **Value Propositions**: What value do you deliver? What problem do you solve?
   - **Channels**: How do you reach and deliver to customers?
   - **Customer Relationships**: How do you acquire, retain, and grow customers?
   - **Revenue Streams**: How do you earn money? Pricing models?
2. Then the left side (infrastructure):
   - **Key Resources**: What assets are essential? (people, IP, infrastructure)
   - **Key Activities**: What must you do well? (development, marketing, ops)
   - **Key Partnerships**: Who do you depend on? (suppliers, integrations, alliances)
   - **Cost Structure**: What are your major costs?
3. **Assess coherence**: Does the left side efficiently support the right side?
4. **Identify risks**: Which blocks are assumptions vs. validated?

## Example
**Product**: Developer documentation platform (SaaS)
- **Customer Segments**: Developer-centric companies (B2B), open-source projects (freemium)
- **Value Propositions**: Beautiful, interactive docs from code; versioning; search; analytics
- **Channels**: Developer communities, content marketing, integrations marketplace
- **Customer Relationships**: Self-serve with community support; enterprise with CSM
- **Revenue Streams**: Tiered SaaS subscriptions; enterprise contracts
- **Key Resources**: Platform infrastructure, engineering team, developer community
- **Key Activities**: Product development, community building, content partnerships
- **Key Partnerships**: CI/CD platforms, code hosting (GitHub/GitLab), CDN providers
- **Cost Structure**: Engineering salaries, cloud infrastructure, developer relations

## Key Pitfalls
- Filling it in once and never updating — the canvas should evolve with your strategy
- Not validating blocks — each block contains assumptions that need testing
- Treating blocks independently — the power is in how they connect
- Making it too detailed — the canvas is meant to be a strategic overview on one page

## Related Frameworks
- **Lean Canvas** — startup-adapted version with Problem, Solution, Unfair Advantage
- **Value Proposition Canvas** — deep dive into Customer Segments and Value Propositions
- **Porter's Five Forces** — competitive context for the BMC
- **Unit Economics** — validates the Revenue Streams and Cost Structure blocks
