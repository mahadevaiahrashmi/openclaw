# Flywheel

## What It Is
The Flywheel model, popularized by Jim Collins and famously applied by Amazon, describes a self-reinforcing growth loop where each component accelerates the next. Unlike linear funnels, flywheels compound over time — the more customers you have, the more value you create, which attracts more customers. The key is identifying the components and understanding how momentum builds.

## When to Use
- Designing or articulating your company's core growth engine
- Identifying which investments compound over time vs. which are one-time
- Strategic planning to find and strengthen the weakest link in your growth loop
- Communicating your competitive moat to investors and leadership

## When NOT to Use
- Early-stage product without clear growth mechanics — find PMF first
- Tactical feature prioritization — the flywheel is strategic
- When growth is linear (e.g., outbound sales only) — not every business has a flywheel

## How to Apply
1. **Identify the core loop**: What sequence of events creates a self-reinforcing cycle?
2. **Map each component**: Usually 4–6 components that feed each other
3. **Identify the energy source**: What gives the flywheel its initial push?
4. **Find the friction points**: What slows the flywheel down?
5. **Identify momentum builders**: What makes each rotation faster than the last?
6. **Invest** in reducing friction and amplifying momentum
7. **Measure** the speed of each component (cycle time, conversion at each step)

## Example
**Amazon's Flywheel**:
1. Lower prices → 2. More customers → 3. More sellers → 4. Wider selection → 5. Better customer experience → 6. More traffic → (back to 1: scale enables lower prices)

**SaaS Example** (Slack):
1. One team adopts → 2. Cross-team collaboration creates value → 3. More teams join → 4. Network effects increase → 5. Company-wide adoption → 6. Enterprise contract → (revenue funds product improvement, attracting more companies)

## Key Pitfalls
- Confusing a funnel with a flywheel — funnels are linear; flywheels loop back
- Too many components — keep it to 4–6 or the loop becomes unclear
- Ignoring friction — the flywheel is only as fast as its slowest/weakest component
- Assuming the flywheel is self-sustaining from day one — it requires significant initial energy

## Related Frameworks
- **Growth Loops** — modern formalization of flywheel concepts
- **Network Effects** — a specific type of flywheel mechanic
- **Product-Led Growth** — flywheel powered by the product itself
- **Unit Economics** — validates that the flywheel is economically sustainable
