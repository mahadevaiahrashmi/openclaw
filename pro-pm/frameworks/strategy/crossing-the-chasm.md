# Crossing the Chasm

## What It Is
Crossing the Chasm, by Geoffrey Moore (1991), identifies a critical gap ("the chasm") in the technology adoption lifecycle between early adopters and the early majority. Early adopters buy on vision; the early majority buys on pragmatic evidence. Most technology products fail in this gap. Moore's solution: focus on a narrow beachhead segment and dominate it before expanding.

## When to Use
- You have early traction with visionaries but struggle to grow beyond them
- Planning your go-to-market strategy for moving from early to mainstream adoption
- When growth stalls after initial excitement and early customers
- Defining your beachhead market and bowling pin strategy

## When NOT to Use
- Pre-product-market-fit — you need early adopters first before worrying about the chasm
- Consumer products with viral mechanics — the chasm is most relevant for B2B/enterprise tech
- Incremental improvements to established products — no chasm to cross

## How to Apply
1. **Acknowledge where you are**: Are you still in early-adopter territory? Signs: customers tolerate bugs, champion internally, don't need references
2. **Identify the chasm**: Early majority requires: whole product, references, proven ROI, low risk
3. **Pick a beachhead segment**: Narrow, specific, desperate for your solution. Must be:
   - Small enough to dominate quickly
   - A compelling reason to buy (pain is acute)
   - Connected to adjacent segments (bowling pin)
4. **Build the Whole Product**: Core product + all the extras needed for mainstream adoption (integrations, support, documentation, professional services)
5. **Create pragmatist-friendly positioning**: Focus on market category, competition, and differentiation — not vision
6. **Execute the Bowling Alley**: Win beachhead → leverage references → expand to adjacent segments

## Example
**Product**: AI code review tool
- Current: Popular with developer influencers and startups (early adopters)
- Beachhead: Mid-size fintech companies with compliance requirements (need automated code review for audit trails)
- Compelling Reason: Regulatory pressure + limited security engineering staff
- Whole Product Gaps: SOC 2 compliance reporting, JIRA integration, SSO
- Bowling Pins: Fintech → Healthcare → Any regulated industry

## Key Pitfalls
- Trying to cross the chasm with a broad market — you must niche down first
- Assuming early adopter enthusiasm will carry you — pragmatists need different proof points
- Skipping the "whole product" — the early majority won't tolerate gaps that early adopters accepted
- Picking a beachhead that's too large or not connected to adjacent segments
