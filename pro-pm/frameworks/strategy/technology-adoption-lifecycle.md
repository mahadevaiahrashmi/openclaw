# Technology Adoption Lifecycle

## What It Is
The Technology Adoption Lifecycle, originated by Everett Rogers (Diffusion of Innovations, 1962), describes how new technologies spread through a population in five segments: Innovators (2.5%), Early Adopters (13.5%), Early Majority (34%), Late Majority (34%), and Laggards (16%). Each group has distinct motivations, risk tolerance, and buying behavior.

## When to Use
- Understanding where your product is in the adoption curve and tailoring strategy accordingly
- Designing go-to-market motions for different adopter segments
- Forecasting adoption timelines and market penetration
- Identifying why growth is stalling (likely at a segment boundary)

## When NOT to Use
- Feature-level decisions — this is a market-level lens
- Products in mature, commoditized markets — the lifecycle has already completed
- When adoption is driven purely by mandate (e.g., regulatory compliance) rather than choice

## How to Apply
1. **Identify your current segment**:
   - **Innovators**: Technology enthusiasts. Buy because it's new. Tolerate rough edges
   - **Early Adopters**: Visionaries. Buy for strategic advantage. Want to be change agents
   - **Early Majority**: Pragmatists. Buy proven solutions. Need references and whole products
   - **Late Majority**: Conservatives. Buy when it's standard. Need simplicity and support
   - **Laggards**: Skeptics. Buy only when forced. Resist change
2. **Assess your product's readiness** for the next segment
3. **Tailor your messaging, pricing, and channel** for each segment
4. **Plan for transitions**, especially the chasm between Early Adopters and Early Majority

## Example
**Product**: No-code automation platform
- **Innovators** (Year 1): Developer advocates building creative automations, sharing on Twitter
- **Early Adopters** (Year 2): Ops leaders at startups using it to replace custom scripts
- **Early Majority** (Year 3–4): Mid-market companies wanting proven workflow automation with SSO, audit logs, and Gartner presence
- Strategy shift needed: From "powerful and flexible" → "trusted and reliable"

## Key Pitfalls
- Marketing to the wrong segment — early majority messaging won't attract innovators, and vice versa
- Assuming adoption is continuous — the chasm between early adopters and early majority is real
- Ignoring that segments have different definitions of "value"
- Treating the percentages as exact — they're directional, not prescriptive

## Related Frameworks
- **Crossing the Chasm** — specific strategies for the early adopter → early majority transition
- **Disruption Theory** — how disruptive products move through the lifecycle from below
- **Product-Led Growth** — can accelerate movement through adoption segments
- **PMF Pyramid** — ensuring readiness before targeting the next segment
