# Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)

## What It Is
JTBD is a theory of customer motivation developed by Clayton Christensen and refined by Tony Ulwick. Instead of focusing on who customers are (demographics), it focuses on what they're trying to accomplish. Customers "hire" products to do a job. Jobs have functional, emotional, and social dimensions and follow a process with discrete steps.

## When to Use
- Defining or redefining your product's core value proposition
- Understanding why customers switch to/from your product
- Identifying innovation opportunities beyond incremental features
- Segmenting customers by behavior and need rather than demographics

## When NOT to Use
- Quick feature prioritization — use RICE after JTBD defines the outcomes
- When you need execution frameworks — JTBD is a strategy/discovery tool
- Optimizing existing flows where usability testing is more appropriate

## How to Apply
1. **Identify the core job**: What is the customer trying to accomplish? Use the format: **[Verb] + [object] + [context]** (e.g., "Stay informed about project status while managing multiple teams")
2. **Map the job steps** (the Universal Job Map):
   - Define → Locate → Prepare → Confirm → Execute → Monitor → Modify → Conclude
3. **Identify desired outcomes** at each step: "Minimize the time it takes to..." or "Minimize the likelihood of..."
4. **Classify job dimensions**:
   - **Functional**: The practical task to accomplish
   - **Emotional**: How the customer wants to feel (personal)
   - **Social**: How the customer wants to be perceived
5. **Find underserved outcomes** using importance vs. satisfaction (Opportunity Scoring)
6. **Design solutions** that nail the underserved outcomes

## Example
**Core Job**: "Help me keep my team aligned on priorities when plans change frequently"
- Functional: Quickly communicate priority changes to the right people
- Emotional: Feel confident that nothing is falling through the cracks
- Social: Be seen as an organized, reliable leader
- Key underserved outcome: "Minimize the time it takes to identify which stakeholders are affected by a priority change"

## Key Pitfalls
- Defining jobs too narrowly (feature-level) or too broadly (life-level) — aim for the middle
- Confusing jobs with solutions — "use a spreadsheet" is a solution, not a job
- Ignoring emotional/social jobs — they often drive switching behavior more than functional jobs
- Treating JTBD as a one-time exercise — jobs evolve as markets and technology change

## Related Frameworks
- **Outcome-Driven Innovation** — Ulwick's quantitative application of JTBD
- **Opportunity Scoring** — measures underserved outcomes from JTBD analysis
- **Value Proposition Canvas** — maps jobs/pains/gains to your offering
- **Disruption Theory** — Christensen's companion theory about market dynamics
