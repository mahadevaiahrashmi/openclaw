# Three Horizons

## What It Is
The Three Horizons framework, popularized by McKinsey (Baghai, Coley, White, 1999), organizes a company's portfolio of initiatives across three time horizons to balance current performance with future growth. H1 sustains the core, H2 scales emerging businesses, and H3 seeds future opportunities.

## When to Use
- Portfolio allocation decisions across innovation investments
- Communicating a balanced strategy to leadership and investors
- Ensuring you're not over-investing in the present at the expense of the future
- Annual strategic planning and R&D budgeting

## When NOT to Use
- Feature-level prioritization — use RICE or WSJF
- When the company is in survival mode — focus on H1 only
- As a rigid time-based framework — horizons are about maturity, not calendar years

## How to Apply
1. **Classify initiatives by horizon**:
   - **H1 — Core Business**: Current products, existing customers, proven business model. Focus: optimize, defend, extend. (70% of resources)
   - **H2 — Emerging Business**: Scaling new products/markets with early traction. Focus: invest to grow, prove scalability. (20% of resources)
   - **H3 — Future Options**: Research, experiments, moonshots. Focus: explore, learn, create options. (10% of resources)
2. **Inventory current investments** and classify each
3. **Assess balance**: Are you over-indexed on H1? Under-investing in H3?
4. **Set explicit resource allocation** targets per horizon
5. **Define success metrics** appropriate to each horizon (H1: revenue/margin, H2: growth rate, H3: learning/options created)
6. **Review quarterly** and graduate projects between horizons

## Example
**Company**: Enterprise analytics platform
- **H1** (70%): Core dashboarding product, existing enterprise customers, feature enhancements, performance improvements
- **H2** (20%): Self-serve analytics for mid-market (launched 6 months ago, growing 30% MoM)
- **H3** (10%): AI-generated insights prototype, natural language query experiment, embedded analytics SDK exploration

## Key Pitfalls
- Treating horizons as sequential time periods — they should run in parallel
- Starving H3 because H1 always has "urgent" needs — protect H3 investment explicitly
- Applying H1 metrics to H3 projects — early experiments should be measured by learning, not revenue
- Keeping projects in H3 forever without graduation criteria — define when to promote or kill

## Related Frameworks
- **Ansoff Matrix** — growth direction that complements horizon allocation
- **Wardley Mapping** — evolution stages that inform which horizon components belong in
- **BCG Matrix** — portfolio classification of existing products
- **Lean Startup** — H3 execution methodology (build-measure-learn for experiments)
