# Bundle Economics

## What It Is
Bundle Economics analyzes when and how to bundle multiple products or features together vs. selling them separately (unbundling). Bundling captures value from heterogeneous customer preferences — customers who value different components all find enough value in the bundle to purchase. The math shows that bundles reduce variance in willingness to pay, enabling higher average revenue.

## When to Use
- Deciding whether to sell products/features together or separately
- Designing product packages for multi-product companies
- Competitive strategy — bundling can be a moat (Microsoft 365) or an attack (unbundle to focus)
- When you have multiple products and want to maximize revenue across them

## When NOT to Use
- Single-product companies with nothing to bundle
- When one product in the bundle is so dominant that the others are perceived as filler
- Markets where customers strongly prefer a la carte purchasing

## How to Apply
1. **Understand bundling types**:
   - **Pure bundling**: Only available as a bundle (cable TV packages)
   - **Mixed bundling**: Available individually AND as a bundle at a discount (Adobe Creative Cloud)
   - **Unbundling**: Selling components separately (Spotify vs. buying albums)
2. **Analyze customer preferences**: Do different customers value different products? If yes, bundling captures more total value
3. **Calculate bundle economics**:
   - If customers have similar preferences → unbundle (they'll all buy the same thing)
   - If customers have diverse preferences → bundle (captures value from different interests)
4. **Set bundle price**: Below the sum of individual prices but above the price of any single component
5. **Test**: A/B test bundle vs. unbundled options to measure revenue impact

## Example
**Company**: Productivity SaaS with 3 products
| Product | Individual | WTP (Cust. A) | WTP (Cust. B) | WTP (Cust. C) |
|---------|-----------|---------------|---------------|---------------|
| Docs | $10 | $15 | $5 | $8 |
| Sheets | $10 | $5 | $12 | $8 |
| Slides | $10 | $8 | $8 | $12 |

**Individual pricing**: Each customer buys only their top-value product. Revenue = $15 + $12 + $12 = $39
**Bundle at $22**: All three customers buy (WTP sum: $28, $25, $28 — all > $22). Revenue = $66
**Result**: Bundling increases revenue by 69% in this scenario

## Key Pitfalls
- Bundling products nobody wants — every component must have some value to the target audience
- Setting bundle price too high (kills conversion) or too low (leaves money on the table)
- Not offering unbundled options for customers who only want one product (mixed bundling is usually best)
- Ignoring cannibalization — a bundle can cannibalize individual product sales

## Related Frameworks
- **Tiered Packaging** — tiers are bundles of features at different price points
- **Feature Gating** — what goes in the bundle vs. what's an add-on
- **Price Anchoring** — showing unbundled sum next to bundle price creates perceived value
- **Competitive Pricing** — competitive bundles set market expectations
