# Penetration vs. Skimming Pricing

## What It Is
Penetration and Skimming are opposing market entry pricing strategies. Penetration pricing enters low to gain market share quickly, then raises prices over time. Skimming enters high to maximize revenue from early adopters willing to pay a premium, then lowers prices as the market matures. The choice depends on competitive dynamics, market elasticity, and strategic goals.

## When to Use
- Launching a new product in a market with established players (penetration to win share)
- Launching an innovative product with no direct competition (skimming to capture willingness to pay)
- Strategic pricing decisions for new markets or new customer segments

## When NOT to Use
- When you have an established product with set pricing — this is for market entry
- When raising prices later is contractually impossible (penetration backfires)
- When the market won't tolerate price changes in either direction

## How to Apply
**Penetration Pricing**:
1. Set price below competitors (typically 20–40% lower)
2. Drive rapid adoption and market share
3. Build switching costs during the low-price period (integrations, data, habits)
4. Gradually raise prices as you establish dominance
5. Requires: low marginal costs, ability to raise prices later, competitive market

**Skimming Pricing**:
1. Launch at premium price targeting early adopters and innovators
2. Capture maximum revenue from those willing to pay the most
3. Gradually lower price (or introduce lower tiers) to reach broader market
4. Each price reduction expands the addressable market
5. Requires: genuine innovation, low competition, strong brand, clear differentiation

## Template
| Factor | Penetration | Skimming |
|--------|------------|---------|

| Competitive response | Preempt or deter entrants | May attract competitors |
| Risk | Can't raise prices; margin pressure | Slow adoption; competitors undercut |
| Best for | Competitive, elastic markets | Novel, inelastic markets |

## Example
**Penetration**: Zoom launched with a generous free tier and low prices ($14.99/host) vs. WebEx ($24.95+) and GoToMeeting ($19+). Gained massive market share during COVID. Now raising prices with established dominance.

**Skimming**: Apple iPhone launched at $599 in 2007 (no competitor). Captured early adopter premium. Over time, introduced SE and older models at lower prices to reach broader market.

## Key Pitfalls
- Penetration without a price-raising plan — you'll be stuck at low prices with thin margins
- Skimming without genuine differentiation — customers will wait for alternatives
- Not considering competitive response — penetration pricing may trigger a price war
- Assuming one strategy fits all segments — you can penetrate in SMB while skimming in enterprise

## Related Frameworks
- **Competitive Pricing** — market context that informs penetration vs. skimming
- **Technology Adoption Lifecycle** — skimming maps to early adopters; penetration to majority
- **Value-Based Pricing** — informs the "right" price that penetration undercuts or skimming reaches
- **Freemium Model** — extreme form of penetration pricing (price = $0 for the free tier)
