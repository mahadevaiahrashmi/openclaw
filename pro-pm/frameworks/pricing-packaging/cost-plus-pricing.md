# Cost-Plus Pricing

## What It Is
Cost-Plus Pricing sets the price by calculating total costs and adding a markup. Price = Cost × (1 + Markup%). It's the simplest pricing method and ensures costs are covered, but it ignores customer value perception and competitive dynamics. Common in manufacturing and services; rarely optimal for software.

## When to Use
- Government contracts or regulated industries requiring cost transparency
- Custom professional services where costs are variable and hard to predict
- Internal transfer pricing between business units
- As a floor/minimum price check against other pricing strategies

## When NOT to Use
- Software/SaaS where marginal cost is near zero — cost-plus dramatically underprices
- Differentiated products where value-based pricing captures more revenue
- Competitive markets where price is set by the market, not your costs

## How to Apply
1. **Calculate total costs**: Fixed costs (salaries, infrastructure, overhead) + Variable costs (hosting, support, transaction fees per customer)
2. **Allocate per unit**: Divide total costs by expected number of customers/units
3. **Add markup**: Industry standard or target margin. Common markups: 20–50% for services, 50–200% for products
4. **Price = Cost per unit × (1 + Markup %)**
5. **Validate**: Check against competitive pricing and willingness to pay

## Example
**Product**: Custom analytics implementation service
- Engineering labor: 80 hours × $150/hr = $12,000
- Infrastructure: $500/month for 6 months = $3,000
- Project management: 20 hours × $100/hr = $2,000
- **Total Cost**: $17,000
- **Markup**: 40% → **Price: $23,800**
- **Reality Check**: Competitors charge $30K–$50K for similar work → cost-plus is leaving $6K–$26K on the table

## Key Pitfalls
- Cost-plus for software is almost always wrong — marginal cost per user is near zero; price on value instead
- Markup percentages that are arbitrary and don't reflect market dynamics
- Ignoring opportunity cost — what else could you do with the resources?
- Cost creep — if costs rise, prices rise, potentially pricing you out of the market
- Using cost-plus as the primary strategy when value-based pricing would yield 2–5x more revenue

## Related Frameworks
- **Value-Based Pricing** — almost always superior to cost-plus for software
- **Competitive Pricing** — market-based alternative to cost-based thinking
- **Unit Economics** — ensures costs are covered regardless of pricing method
- **Penetration vs. Skimming** — strategic pricing that cost-plus doesn't account for
