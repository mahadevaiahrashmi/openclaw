# Value-Based Pricing

## What It Is
Value-Based Pricing sets prices based on the value delivered to the customer, not the cost to produce or what competitors charge. It requires understanding the economic benefit your product creates and capturing a fair share of it. It's the most profitable pricing strategy when executed well, but requires deep customer understanding.

## When to Use
- Products with clear, measurable economic value to the customer
- Differentiated products where competitive pricing would leave money on the table
- Enterprise products where ROI can be calculated
- When cost-plus pricing significantly undervalues your product

## When NOT to Use
- Commodity products where customers have many equivalent alternatives
- Products where value is subjective or hard to quantify
- Markets where price transparency forces competitive parity

## How to Apply
1. **Quantify customer value**: What economic benefit does your product create?
   - Time saved × hourly rate = $X
   - Revenue increased = $Y
   - Costs avoided = $Z
   - Total Economic Value (TEV) = X + Y + Z
2. **Determine your capture rate**: Price = TEV × capture rate (typically 10–30% for software)
3. **Anchor to the next best alternative**: What would the customer pay/do without you?
4. **Differentiation value**: Your price = Reference price + value of differentiation - cost of switching
5. **Segment**: Different customer segments get different value; price accordingly
6. **Validate**: Use Van Westendorp or conjoint to check that your value-based price falls within acceptable range

## Example
**Product**: Automated compliance monitoring for fintech
- Time savings: 20 hrs/month of manual compliance work × $75/hr = $18,000/year
- Cost avoidance: Average compliance fine = $250K × 5% probability reduction = $12,500/year
- Revenue enablement: Faster audit completion → 2 weeks faster enterprise deal closing = $50K/year
- **TEV**: ~$80,000/year
- **Capture rate**: 15% → Suggested price: $12,000/year
- **Reference price**: Manual compliance consultant = $48,000/year → strong value proposition

## Key Pitfalls
- Overestimating value — be conservative; customers will challenge inflated claims
- Not connecting price to measurable outcomes — "it saves time" is weak; "it saves 20 hours/month" is strong
- Uniform pricing when value varies by segment — enterprise gets more value than SMB; price differently
- Ignoring willingness to pay — even if value is $100K, customers may not pay $15K if they don't believe the calculation

## Related Frameworks
- **Conjoint Analysis** — validates willingness to pay for specific value components
- **Cost-Plus Pricing** — the simpler but usually suboptimal alternative
- **Unit Economics** — ensures your value-based price produces healthy LTV:CAC
- **Van Westendorp** — checks whether your value-based price falls in the acceptable range
