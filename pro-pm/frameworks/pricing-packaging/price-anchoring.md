# Price Anchoring

## What It Is
Price Anchoring uses cognitive biases — primarily anchoring bias — to influence how customers perceive your pricing. The first price a customer sees becomes the "anchor" against which all subsequent prices are evaluated. This includes decoy pricing, charm pricing, and strategic bundling to make your target option feel like the best value.

## When to Use
- Designing pricing pages to drive conversion to a specific tier
- When customers lack a reference point for what your product "should" cost
- Increasing perceived value of your recommended option
- Competitive positioning where you want to frame the comparison favorably

## When NOT to Use
- When customers have strong price reference points from competitors (anchor is already set)
- Transparent, trust-critical relationships where psychological tactics feel manipulative
- Regulated pricing environments

## How to Apply
1. **High anchor**: Present the most expensive option first (or prominently). This makes lower options feel like bargains
2. **Decoy effect**: Add a third option that's intentionally unattractive to make your target option look better
   - Example: Small ($5), Medium ($12), Large ($15) — Medium looks bad vs. Large, pushing people to Large
3. **Charm pricing**: Prices ending in 9 ($29 vs. $30) — creates a "just under" perception
4. **Strikethrough pricing**: Show original price crossed out with discounted price — creates value perception
5. **Frame the comparison**: "Less than a cup of coffee per day" or "1/10th the cost of hiring a full-time analyst"
6. **Bundle anchoring**: Show the unbundled price ($50 + $30 + $20 = $100) next to the bundle price ($69) — creates perceived savings

## Template
| Technique | Implementation | Target Effect |
|-----------|---------------|--------------|
| High anchor | Highest tier at $___/mo shown first | Makes ___/mo tier feel reasonable |
| Decoy | Option at $___ with [features] | Pushes toward $___ option |
| Framing | "Only $___/day" or "Save $___/year" | Reduces perceived magnitude |
| Comparison | "vs. $___ for [alternative]" | Creates value perception |

## Example
**Product**: Email marketing platform pricing page
- **Decoy setup**: Starter ($29/month, 5K contacts), Growth ($79/month, 25K contacts), Scale ($89/month, 50K contacts + automation)
- **Effect**: Growth at $79 looks poor compared to Scale at $89 (10x contacts + automation for just $10 more) → 62% of buyers choose Scale
- **Framing**: "Scale plan = $2.97/day — less than your morning coffee"
- **High anchor**: Enterprise starts at $499/month (shown first) → makes $89 feel accessible

## Key Pitfalls
- Anchoring that feels deceptive — customers who feel manipulated don't return
- Decoy that's too obviously a decoy — sophistication matters
- Not testing — the effectiveness of anchoring varies by audience; A/B test
- Using anchoring as a substitute for genuine value — it enhances perception but can't fix a bad product

## Related Frameworks
- **Tiered Packaging** — the primary context where anchoring is applied
- **Competitive Pricing** — competitor prices become external anchors
- **Van Westendorp** — reveals what prices feel "too cheap" or "too expensive" (natural anchors)
- **Bundle Economics** — bundling creates anchoring through price comparison
