# Podcast Talking Points

## Purpose
Prepares a guest appearance brief with key messages, stories, soundbites, bridging phrases, Q&A preparation, and a call to action. Conversational and flexible—not a script, but a preparation framework that ensures the speaker delivers value while staying on message.

## Auto-Trigger Patterns
- "Prepare podcast talking points for [show/topic]"
- "Create a podcast guest prep brief"
- "I need to prepare for a podcast interview about [topic]"
- "Help me get ready for a podcast appearance"

## Inputs

**Zero-setup:** Only the user prompt is required. If context files are empty, use `context/_defaults.md` and label assumptions. See `skills/_GLOBAL-BEHAVIOR.md`.

- **Required:** Podcast name/format, topic, key messages (3-4), speaker's expertise and stories
- **Optional:** Host's style, past episodes for reference, audience demographics, episode angle
- **Reference files:** product-brief.md, blog-post-thought-leadership.md, conference-talk-deck.md

## Process
1. **Research the podcast** — Listen to 2-3 recent episodes. Note host's style, question patterns, and audience.
2. **Define key messages** — 3-4 points you want the listener to remember. Prioritize.
3. **Prepare stories** — Every key message needs a story or example that makes it memorable.
4. **Write soundbites** — Quotable phrases that capture your insight in 10 seconds.
5. **Build bridges** — Phrases to redirect off-topic questions back to your key messages.
6. **Prepare for tough questions** — Honest, concise answers to uncomfortable questions.

## Output Format
```
# Podcast Prep: [Podcast Name] — [Episode Topic]

## Podcast Context
- **Format:** [Interview, panel, solo] | **Duration:** [X minutes]
- **Host:** [Name, style notes]
- **Audience:** [Who listens, what they care about]
- **Angle:** [What the host wants to explore]

## Key Messages (ranked by priority)
1. **[Message 1]:** [Full articulation]
   Story: [Anecdote that illustrates this point]
   Soundbite: "[10-second quotable version]"

2. **[Message 2]:** [Same structure]
3. **[Message 3]:** [Same structure]

## Stories Bank
- **Origin story:** [How you got into this field/built this product]
- **Failure story:** [Something that went wrong and what you learned]
- **Customer story:** [Anonymized example of impact]
- **Counterintuitive story:** [Something surprising the audience won't expect]

## Bridging Phrases
- "That's a great question, and it connects to something I've been thinking about..."
- "What I've found in practice is actually different from what you'd expect..."
- "The way I think about it is..."
- "One thing people miss about this is..."

## Anticipated Questions & Answers
Q: [Likely question 1]
A: [Concise, story-backed answer]

Q: [Likely question 2-5]
A: [Same structure]

## CTA (natural, not salesy)
"If this resonated, [specific action]. You can find [resource] at [URL]."

## Dos and Don'ts
✅ Be conversational, not rehearsed
✅ Use specific examples over general advice
✅ Acknowledge what you don't know
❌ Don't pitch the product unprompted
❌ Don't give one-word answers
❌ Don't dominate—match the host's energy
```

## Quality Standards
- Key messages are memorable enough that a listener driving a car would retain them.
- Stories are specific, personal, and relevant—not corporate case studies.
- Soundbites are quotable and shareable on social media.
- **Anti-patterns:** Rehearsed-sounding delivery, product pitching, generic advice, no stories, refusing to be vulnerable.

## Audience
Podcast listeners who are practitioners, founders, or leaders in the field. They listen for insights, stories, and frameworks they can apply. They remember how the guest made them feel more than specific data points.

## Formatting Guidelines
- Brief is 1-2 pages. Not a script—a preparation tool.
- Stories in 60-second versions (can expand if host engages).
- Soundbites under 15 words.
- Include podcast-specific context (host name, recent episode references).

## Example
**Key Message 1:** "The best products aren't built by the best engineers—they're built by teams that talk to customers every single week."
Story: "When I joined Acme, engineering had shipped 40 features in a quarter and usage was flat. The first thing I did was cancel all internal meetings for a week and have every PM do 5 customer calls. Within two weeks, we killed 3 in-progress features and redirected that effort. That quarter, we shipped 12 features instead of 40—and usage grew 35%."
Soundbite: "We went from shipping 40 features nobody wanted to 12 features everyone needed."
